# 4. Aerial Incident of 8 January 2020

(Canada, Sweden, Ukraine and United Kingdom v. Islamic Republic of Iran) Joint Application 4 July 2023 No. 190

## Introduction

On 3 July 2023, Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom jointly filed a case before the ICJ against the Islamic Republic of Iran. The four nations accused Iran of violating the Convention on the Suppression of Unlawful Acts Against Civil Aviation following the tragic downing of Flight PS752, a civilian aircraft en route to Kyiv (Joint Application Instituting Proceedings: Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 2023), which was shot down shortly after take-off from Tehran.

This case marks a pivotal moment in international legal efforts to hold states accountable for civilian aviation safety in conflict zones, highlighting ongoing calls for clearer global standards to protect air travel in such dangerous contexts (UN expert calls for clear, unambiguous standards to protect aircraft over conflict zones. 2021).

## Historical/Political Context

Tensions between Iran and the US were especially heightened in the weeks before the downing of flight PS752. In December 2019, an Iraqi militia, backed by the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)’s Quds Force, attacked an Iraqi air base hosting U.S. personnel, leading the US to conduct air strikes against that militia  (Martin, R. 2025). On January 3, 2020, a U.S. drone strike killed the leader of that militia and the commander of the Quds Force, Qassem Soleimani, as they met at Baghdad International Airport.

On the morning of January 8, Iranian forces launched ballistic missiles at two bases housing and training U.S.-led forces in Iraq. Both the assassination and the missile strike took place in Iraq. Expectations in Iran that morning were that the Americans would retaliate, and Iranian forces were put on high alert in preparation. Some days after the plane crash, the IRGC apologised to the nation and accepted full responsibility for the plane crash, declaring it a “disastrous mistake” but blaming the US for heightened tension (Protests in Tehran after Iran admits shooting down plane, 2020).

This statement triggered another wave of Iranian anti-government protests, calling for the removal of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. In Tehran, protesters chanted slogans denouncing “liars” and demanded the resignation and prosecution of those responsible for downing the plane and allegedly covering up the accidental action (Protests in Tehran after Iran admits shooting down plane, 2020). Foreign Secretary Dominic Raab said the arrest was a “flagrant violation of international law” and repeated calls for Iran to de-escalate tensions. The disaster echoed a 1988 incident, when a US warship shot down an Iranian airliner, killing 290 people. Washington claimed it was an accident, in turn, Tehran said it was intentional (Protests in Tehran after Iran admits shooting down plane, 2020).

## Case Facts

While the following explanation is technical in nature, such precision is necessary for a thorough understanding of the incident's dynamics. On 8 January 2020, Iran took a series of actions that ultimately put the civilian aircraft PS752 in serious danger (Joint Application Instituting Proceedings: Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 2023).

In the early hours of the morning, around 2:00 a.m., the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched ballistic missiles at U.S. and coalition bases in Iraq. Anticipating a possible counterattack, the IRGC positioned surface-to-air missile systems around Tehran, including near Imam Khomeini International Airport. One such system was stationed in Bidganeh, along routine civilian flight paths.

Despite recognizing the high risk to international civil aviation, Iranian authorities chose not to close the airspace. A senior military officer, Brigadier General Amir Ali Hajizadeh, had reportedly requested that the airspace be shut down, but that request was denied. Compounding the danger, Iran also failed to issue international flight safety warnings during heightened military activity, known as NOTAMs, which would have alerted pilots and airlines to the threat.

Later that morning, at 5:51 a.m., Flight PS752 received clearance to start its engines and, shortly thereafter, was approved for take off. At 6:12 a.m., the plane departed as planned. Less than three minutes into its flight, the IRGC launched a surface-to-air missile at the aircraft. A second missile followed seconds later, and the Cockpit Voice Recorder (CVR) captured a “strong and short impulse” consistent with detonation. Shrapnel from the explosions pierced the plane’s fuselage, and passengers remained conscious in the final moments as the aircraft descended. The plane disappeared from radar at 6:17 a.m. and crashed minutes later near the town of Khalajabad in Shahedshahr, southwest of Tehran.

In the immediate aftermath, Iranian officials denied any responsibility, claiming the crash was caused by a technical failure. These denials continued for several days, even as satellite data and intelligence reports from other countries began to point clearly to a missile strike. Finally, on 11 January 2020, after intense international pressure and public condemnation, Iran admitted that its forces had shot down Flight PS752. The explanation given was “human error”, an acknowledgement that came only after widespread outrage and scrutiny.

## Misconduct of Iran

Hours after the crash, Iran bulldozed the crash site, compromising forensic investigations. Families seeking justice were threatened and harassed by Iranian authorities - some had their passports confiscated or faced intimidation. Moreover, Iran’s Aircraft Accident Investigation Board (AAIB) released a report blaming low-ranking IRGC personnel while omitting critical systemic failures. The subsequent trial lacked transparency and accountability for senior officials. Furthermore, Iran did not provide timely information to the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) or affected states regarding the offense or measures taken against offenders (Joint Application Instituting Proceedings: Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 2023).

## Legal Issues/questions of the Court

Did Iran violate its obligations to Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom under the Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts against the Safety of Civil Aviation (Montreal Convention) by shooting down Ukraine International Airlines Flight PS752 on January 8, 2020?

Specifically, did Iran fail to take all practicable measures to prevent the unlawful and intentional commission of an offense described in Article 1 of the Montreal Convention, including the destruction of Flight PS752 (Article 6)?

Did Iran fail to conduct an impartial, transparent, and fair criminal investigation and prosecution consistent with international law (Articles 7, 10, 11, and 13)?

Did Iran withhold or destroy evidence, blame other states and low-level IRGC military personnel, threaten and harass the families of the victims, conduct a sham and opaque trial, and fail to report information to the ICA or the Applicant states?

## Position of Parties

The countries bringing the case, Canada, Sweden, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom, are asking the ICJ to hold Iran accountable under international law. Specifically, they argue that Iran violated its obligations under the Montreal Convention, a treaty designed to protect civilian aviation. These applicants are asking the Court to declare Iran in breach of this convention, to order Iran to fully compensate the victims’ families and affected states, and to require Iran to carry out a transparent investigation and prosecution that meets international legal standards.

Iran, for its part, initially denied any responsibility, suggesting the crash of Flight PS752 was due to a technical failure. Days later, it admitted that the plane was shot down by its own forces but maintained that the strike was an unintentional human error. Iran argued that its military was on high alert following its missile attacks on U.S. bases in Iraq earlier that night and believed that a U.S. counterstrike was imminent.
