# Current Cases of the ICJ - How the Court Shapes our Future

*Aliya Sara Akdogan, Erin Behlic, Marie-Julie Cantraine, Monica García-Salmones, Hiella Gawhari, Flora Hapkemeyer, Mihnea Pînzaru, Sherife Su Mehmetaliogullari, Sofia Naryzhnyaya, Nela Rozdalovska, Elide Saia, Brina Samardzija, Julie Subke*

## Introduction

Pending cases or contentious cases refer to legal proceedings that are ongoing but have not yet been finalized or resolved, or legal proceedings relating to a dispute or controversy. Unlike historical cases, whose outcomes deeply shaped the functions and the dynamics of international law, we do not yet know what will be the outcome of the pending or recently resolved cases and advisory opinions that appear in this chapter. But we can already affirm that they illustrate how the ICJ continues to play its role in ongoing geopolitical issues as the principal judicial organ of the United Nations.

These modern cases reflect both the broadening scope of international legal challenges and the shifting nature of global relations. They span a diverse range of issues, including allegations of genocide in Gaza, human rights abuses in Syria, climate change responsibilities, ethnic discrimination in the Caucasus, and disputes over whaling practices. Together, they demonstrate how the ICJ remains central to interpreting and applying international law in response to urgent and contested matters on the world stage.

The diversity in these cases reflects not only the ICJ's broad mandate but also the growing significance of international law in shaping global political discourse. States are increasingly turning to the Court as a mechanism for accountability, diplomatic leverage, and the enforcement of international norms.

This can be seen in the climate change advisory opinion, initiated by developing states led by Vanuatu, which portrays how international law amplifies the voices of vulnerable nations. Similarly, the pending case against Afghanistan highlights how multiple states can utilise treaty obligations to challenge human rights violations. Furthermore, cases such as the environmental concerns regarding whaling and territorial conflicts in the Armenia-Azerbaijan case, exemplify the need for international legal intervention when a state's policies have an impact beyond national borders. The South Africa v. Israel case, in particular, shows how universal (_erga omnes_) obligations under international conventions, such as the prohibition of genocide, can be invoked by third-party states against conflicts, even if they are not directly involved, demonstrating the increase of collective responsibility for fundamental rights.

There is a generally consistent pattern that the ICJ follows, with small variations. In contentious cases, one state files an application for a case against another. This will be followed by a period of preliminary questioning in regard to the Court's jurisdiction or to the validity of the claims that were made. If the case proceeds, the core legal issues will be addressed in the merits phase.

 In urgent cases, provisional measures can be issued by the Court to prevent further damage from occurring during case proceedings. Contrarily, Advisory proceedings are initiated by UN organs seeking the Court's opinion regarding legal questions. The time that the Court might take to resolve cases and Advisory proceedings varies much.

This section dealing with key ICJ's contemporary cases illustrates how international law faces the most urgent issues of today: genocide, climate change, and abuse of human rights. Whilst the Court may face limitations with enforcement, these cases show that international law serves to challenge injustice and promote global commitment to fundamental rights.
