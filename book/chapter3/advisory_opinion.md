# 1. Advisory Opinion on the Obligations of States in Respect of Climate Change

_Obligations of States in respect of Climate Change_ (Advisory Opinion) (International Court of Justice, General List No 187, 23 July 2025) [456]

## Introduction

An advisory opinion is the non-binding interpretation of law of the ICJ on legal questions referred to it. Only organs and specialised agencies of the UN can ask the ICJ for advisory opinions. States are not allowed to do so. However, once a request has been made, states are invited or asked to give their view on the situation. This can be done in writing or orally.  Even though advisory opinions are not binding in nature, they are legally and morally highly authoritative. Their purpose is to clarify questions regarding the interpretation of international law and to give the ICJ’s opinion and guidance about possible legal consequences of a situation presented to it.

## Case Background

The request for an advisory opinion is in the situation at hand rooted in the existential threat climate change poses, especially for Small Island Developing States (SIDS) like Vanuatu. These states are already facing severe and escalating impacts: rising sea levels, extreme weather events, threats to biodiversity, and damage to the health and livelihood of their populations. (Jahn & Letsch, 2025)

These effects are scientifically linked to anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions and are projected to worsen, placing a disproportionate burden on vulnerable states with limited adaptive capacity. Through the request, the petitioners seek legal clarity and accountability to guide international efforts and promote environmental peace.

## Admissibility and Procedural Context

The first two questions the International Court of Justice must answer when asked to issue an advisory opinion are whether the request is admissible and whether the ICJ has jurisdiction to respond. Since individual States are not permitted to request advisory opinions directly, they have to persuade an authorized United Nations organ, such as the UN General Assembly or the Security Council, to submit the request to the ICJ under Article 96 of the UN Charter (International Court of Justice, 2023, para. 8).

After a year of campaigning by Vanuatu and over a year of global consultations, the General Assembly ultimately adopted the resolution. UN member states as well as over 1.500 civil society and youth groups across 130 countries participated in the informal consultation process. On March 29, 2023, the resolution was adopted by consensus, and on April 19th 2023 the General Assembly formally referred it to the ICJ (para. 1; Bañuelos, 2023).

Ultimately, the Court itself decides whether it has jurisdiction to consider the matter. This principle known as “competence de la competence” is enshrined in Article 65 (1) of the ICJ Statute. Only legal questions may be referred to the Court which retains the discretion to reformulate and interpret them. Once the Court approved of the request, States and organizations were invited to submit written statements and make commentaries on the submissions of others (Bañuelos, 2023).

Following Article 66 (2) of the ICJ Statute, the initial deadline for written submissions
was October 20, 2023. As per Article 66 (4) the time-limit for comments on those submissions was set for January 22, 2024 (International Court of Justice, 2023a, para. 2). Both deadlines were extended twice to a total extension period of six months, first on August 9, 2023 and again on December 19, 2023 (International Court of Justice, 2023b, para. 1; International Court of Justice, 2023c, para. 1).

By April 12, 2024, the Registry had received 91 written statements, the highest number of written statements ever submitted in advisory opinions before the Court. Following a third extension, 62 written comments had been filed by August 15, 2024 (International Court of Justice, 2024a, para. 1). The International Court of Justice held public hearings from 2 December to 13 December 2024 at the Peace Palace in The Hague, the seat of the Court. A total of 96 States and 11 international organizations delivered oral statements (International Court of Justice, 2024b, paras. 1-2).

## The Legal Questions before the Court

The United Nations General Assembly (UNGA) requested the Court to address the following two legal questions in Resolution 77/276 ([A/RES/77/276 General Assembly](https://docs.un.org/en/A/RES/77/276); 2023, p. 3):

1.  What are the obligations of states under international law to ensure the protection of the climate system and other parts of the environment from anthropogenic emissions of greenhouse gases for present and future generations?

2.  What are the legal consequences under these obligations for states that, by their acts or omissions, have caused significant harm to the climate system and other parts of the environment, particularly in relation to:

1.  States that are injured or specifically affected by the adverse effects of climate change, especially Small Island Developing States (SIDS)?
2.  Peoples and individuals of present and future generations affected by the climate change?

## Parties’ Submissions and Legal Arguments

### Petitioning States (e.g. Vanuatu, SIDS, Least Developed Countries) (Lawrence, 2024, p. 285)

1.  States have binding legal obligations to mitigate and adapt to climate change under international environmental law. (Foster, 2024, pp. 590-591)

2.  The “no harm” principle, which prohibits states from causing transboundary environmental damage, is being violated by the actions or inaction of major emitters. (Lawrence, 2024, p. 291)

3.  Urgent and effective action is required to limit greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in order to protect the rights of the current and future generations and fulfill obligations under treaties such as the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the Paris Agreements, as well as customary international law and general principles. (Lawrence, 2024, pp. 291-292)

### Responding States (e.g. United States, Saudi Arabia, Others)

1.  While climate change agreements like the Paris Agreement set important goals, they consist primarily of voluntary commitments, and do not impose uniform, binding legal obligations. (Lawrence, 2024, p. 299)

2.  The principle of “Common But Differentiated Responsibilities” (CBDR) should guide interpretation, meaning states’ obligations must reflect their respective capabilities, development levels, and historical contributions to climate change. (Lawrence, 2024, pp. 295-296)

3.  Some states, such as major fossil fuels producers, also expressed concern over the economic consequences that a binding legal framework might entail.

Other major GHG emitters such as China and India adopted more nuanced positions. They have supported global climate efforts but also emphasized equity and differentiated responsibility, pointing out that developed countries have historically emitted the bulk of greenhouse gases. While they have acknowledged the importance of addressing climate change, they have argued that historical emissions and varying capacities should influence the extent of each nation's obligations. (India, pp. 12, paras. 2-3; China, p. 8, para. 13)

Saudi Arabia, as one of the major oil-producing nations of the world, also expressed concerns since their economic interests would suffer in case of a binding legal framework. (Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, p. 3, para. 1.5 i) The United States and Saudi Arabia have expressed reservations about the advisory opinion, relating to the argument that international agreements such as the Paris Agreement are sufficient to address climate change.

## Advisory Opinion

Just out of the oven, the ICJ gave its Advisory Opinion on the Obligations of States on Climate Change on 23 July 2025. Experts already regard the Court’s statement a landmark Advisory Opinion.

The Court considered that the laws applicable to the issue are the main climate change agreements and general customary principles of international law. An example of the former is the Paris Agreement, and of the latter, norms such as the duty to prevent significant harm to the environment and the duty to cooperate for the protection of the environment and human rights law; the Court also considers other core principles, such as ‘(sustainable development, common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities, equity, intergenerational equity, and the precautionary approach or principle)’, and other general norms of international law (_Obligations of States in respect of Climate Change_, 23 July 2025, para. 172).

One may state in other words, that the law that the ICJ has found to be applicable to the issue of climate change is not only specialised law (_lex specialis_) (para. 171), neither law only applicable to the parties to the climate change agreements but to any State, as customary obligations obliged every State in the world (para. 315).

Moreover, with regard to the law applicable in cases of specific breaches of these norms, the Court has determined that general norms of state responsibility under customary law apply, since it did not find that the climate change agreement possess mechanism to uphold that responsibility (para. 420).

The Court declares that the main climate change agreements contain legally binding obligations of conduct and result. These concentrate in mitigation of Green House Gases (GHG), which includes maritime pollution (para. 340), protection of sinks and reservoirs (parts of the climate system that enable GHG to be stored such as woods and jungles) (paras 200; 202; 204; 207), adaptation to climate change (para. 209), and co-operation (para. 262). In particular the Court considers that there is an obligation of transferring technology and financial resources from developed to developing countries to help the latter in their efforts against GHG (para. 264).

Importantly, the Court has recalled the obligation enshrined in the Paris Agreement, art. 2, paragraph 1, of setting a common cap limit to the rise of temperature globally (para. 223):

> ‘a) holding the increase in the global average temperature to well
> below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit
> the temperature increase to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels,
> recognizing that this would significantly reduce the risks and impacts
> of climate change’.

According to the Advisory Opinion, States are obliged to communicate the nationally determined contributions (NDC) to achieve the temperature goal (para. 234) and progression of contributions, which ought to be ‘ambitious’ (para. 242) and following ‘stringent standards’ (para. 246). Thus, the overall objectives of the NDC are according to the Court to achieve the temperature goal and “stabilization of greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system” (para. 245).

Applying the common but differentiated principle, the ICJ establishes (in this and other issues) a difference between _developed countries_ that ought to take the lead in gas emission reductions, and _developing countries_, expected to mitigate according to their capabilities and move according to their circumstances towards an economy-wide emission reduction. (paras. 246; 248). However, the Court considers that each State shall act with a level of due diligence, employing ‘all means at its disposal’ to prevent a significant harm to the environment. (paras. 280; 292).

Finally, despite the complexity of the GHG phenomenon, usually the result of collective and aggregate actions and effects, (para 421.) the Court declares that failure to protect the climate system from GHG emissions may constitute a wrongful act attributable to a State (para. 427). The Court further affirms that it is scientifically possible to determine the emissions of a State in a determined time (para. 429). Finally, the Court affirms that ‘States’ obligations pertaining to the protection of the climate system and other parts of the environment from anthropogenic GHG emissions, in particular the obligation to prevent significant transboundary harm under customary international law are obligations _erga omnes_’ (para. 440). It also affirms the duties of performance, of cessation and guarantees of non-repetition and the duty to make reparation. (paras. 446; 447; 449)

## Potential Outcome and Impact

Even though advisory opinions are characterized by not producing legally binding effects on States, the latter cannot ignore the fundamental rules that the Court underlines. States can also not ignore the legal consequences that will follow their violations of the law and principles established in the Advisory Opinion. In other words, advisory opinions can still influence the future decision making or policies of countries since, as opinions of the ICJ, they are highly authoritative.
