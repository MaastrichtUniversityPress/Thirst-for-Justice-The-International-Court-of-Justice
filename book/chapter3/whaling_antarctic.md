# 2. Whaling in the Antarctic

(Australia v. Japan: New Zealand intervening, 2014)

## Facts

By the time commercial whaling arrived in Antarctica at roughly the turn of the 20th century, whalers had become significantly more efficient thanks to technological innovations such as the exploding harpoon. Further advances would significantly expand the capabilities of whaling operations in just a couple of decades. Therefore, it so happened that whaling kicked off in Antarctica right around when it was becoming as lethally effective as it had ever been. The end result was drastic collapses in the populations of most Southern Ocean whales (Antarctica Cruises, n.d).

As the 1920s arrived, international concern was mounting over the sustainability of commercial whaling. In 1930, the Bureau of International Whaling Statistics was established to monitor the industry. In 1931, 22 nations signed the Convention for the Regulation of Whaling. However, several major whaling countries were not part of the original signatories. In 1948, the International Whaling Commission was created as the decision-making body of the new International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling which was signed in 1946 (International Whaling Commission, 1946). According to this new convention, Blue whales now received international protection in 1965, and by the late 1970s the International Whaling Commission had also banned commercial whaling of fin and sei whales in the Southern Ocean. Against this backdrop of continued regulation the state of Japan issued their first JARPA (Japan’s research whaling in the Antarctic) in the 1987-1988 season and the subsequent JARPA II with the purposes of: monitoring the Antarctic ecosystem, modelling competition among whale species and future management objectives, elucidating temporal and spatial changes in stock structure and improving the management procedure for Antarctic minke whale stocks (Institute of Cetacean Research, n.d).

The Japanese government deemed necessary for this scientific research to authorize the annual killing of 850 minke whales, 50 fin whales and 50 humpback whales within the Southern Ocean Sanctuary. Therefore, on 31st of May 2010, Australia instituted proceedings against the state of Japan at the ICJ for alleged breaches of Article 8 paragraph 1 of the International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling (International Court of Justice, 2010).

## The Question before the Court

The court had to consider whether Japan’s JARPA II program for scientific whaling was conducted for actual scientific research and good faith or if it had violated the rules of International Law outlined in the International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling (ICWR) (para. 42).

## Applicant’s Claim

Australia argued that by issuing a special permit for Japan's JARPA II program which would be designated just for scientific purposes, the state of Japan was disguising commercial whaling masquerading as a scientific study (International Court of Justice, 2011). This is due to the fact that in Japan’s alleged scientific program a very high number of annual killings would be permitted in order to achieve the goals of its study. Moreover, Australia argued that due to the high limit of the capture of whales, Japan’s JARPA II program was actually promoting the killing and commercializing of whales which is in violation of the ICRW and the 1994 Southern Ocean Whale Sanctuary, both significant International Law legal documents(Ch. 5, Sec. 3).

## Defendant’s Claim

Japan had persisted that its whaling, in the form of the JARPA II program, was conducted for the purposes of genuine scientific research only as permitted by International Law itself and was not intended to be considered as commercial. Japan had contended that the program was intended to gather data for the future management of whale stock and to enhance whale biology knowledge.

## The Intervention of New Zealand

New Zealand joined Australia in its arguments, emphasizing the need to maintain whale populations and questioning the scientific character of Japan's whaling activity.

## The Holding of the Court

On the 31st of March 2014 the court issued its judgement (International Court of Justice, 2014). The International court of Justice found Japan guilty of breaching the International Convention on the Regulation of Whaling. The court found that Japan’s JARPA II program did not constitute legitimate scientific research. In that sense, the court concluded that the scale, methods and scope of JARPA II were not reasonable in relation to the goals of scientific research, and that Japan did not demonstrate the justification of the high number of killings in achieving the objectives of its own program (para. 224). The court also ordered Japan to cease the JARPA II program immediately and refrain from issuing permits regarding whaling in the Southern Ocean. Furthermore, in the judgement, the court emphasized the need for scientific whaling programs to align with the ICRW’s stipulation of being actually scientific and that they should include research objectives that are necessary for the proper conservation of the whale species (para. 227).

## Legal Effect

Following the ICJ’s ruling, Japan officially stated it would halt its whaling activities in the Southern Ocean for the time being. However, Japan later announced plans to continue whaling in the Antarctic, claiming that they would do so under a new, revised program that adhered more closely to the ICJ’s ruling (Hodgson-Johnston & Jabour, 2014).

The case remains a landmark in international environmental and conservation laws as it set a number of precedents. Firstly, for the first time in the history of the International Court of Justice, the court legally defined what “Scientific Research” actually means. Secondly, the case actually set a trend for the court in regards to how it interprets International law, leading to a trend toward sustainability, conservation and ecological responsibility towards the states themselves. Finally, and probably most importantly, the court emphasized the fact that environmental issues, such as whaling in our case, are collective concerns. In our case this meant that even if Japan had a right to hunt those whales for scientific purposes, their actions negatively impacted the environmental state of the entire world. The Court thus had to order Japan to stop for the good of the environment.
