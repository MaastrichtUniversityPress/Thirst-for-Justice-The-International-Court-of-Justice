# 7. Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in the Gaza Strip (South Africa v. Israel).

_ICJ General List No. 192, Filed 29 December 2023_

## Political-Historical Background

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict, one of world’s most prolonged and violent disputes, can be traced back to the late 19th century, when waves of Jewish migration to Ottoman-controlled Palestine began, largely as a response to pogroms and persecution in Eastern Europe and in connection with the rise of Zionism (Morris, 2003). Some historians argue that the conflict intensified with the United Nations’ 1947 vote to partition the British Mandate of Palestine into two states, Jewish and Arab, following the destruction of much of European Jewry in the Holocaust (Britannica, 2025).

In 1948, the State of Israel declared its independence. During the ensuing Arab-Israeli war, Israel expanded its control to 77% of the territory of Mandatory Palestine, including the western Jerusalem  (Pappe, 2006). Over half of the Palestinian Arab population either fled or were forcibly expelled. This mass displacement is referred to as the _Nakba_ or “catastrophe” by Palestinians (Khalidi, 1997).

To this day, there has been little to no progress towards an agreement under Israel’s longest-serving prime minister, Benjamin Netanyahu, who has repeatedly opposed the establishment of a sovereign Palestinian state (Staff, 2015)  . In 2024, the International Criminal Court issued warrants of arrest for Netanyahu and Yoav Gallant (ex-minister of defense), for crimes against humanity and war crimes committed from at least 8 October 2023 until at least 20 May 2024 (ICC, 2024).

This case represents an endless and cyclical conflict between the two populations and how the international community reacted to Israel’s actions towards the Palestinian people, recognizing acts of genocide.

## Case Facts

On 29 December 2023, South Africa instituted proceedings against Israel before ICJ, concerning alleged violations of the 1948 Genocide Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide. South Africa claimed that the military operations conducted by the State of Israel in the Gaza Strip_,_ in the wake of 7 October 2023 attacks in Israel, constituted genocidal acts carried out with a specific intent of destroying Palestinians, as a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group, in Gaza (Application Instituting Proceedings containing a request for the indication of Provisonal Measures, 2023).

Article II of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide defines genocide as  any of the following acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group, as such:

1. Killing members of the group;

2. Causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group;

3. Deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part;

4. Imposing measures intended to prevent births within the group;

5. Forcibly transferring children of the group to another group.

South Africa simultaneously requested the indication of provisional measures under Article 41 of the ICJ Statute to minimize further escalations and prevent further harm whilst the proceedings were pending. Public hearings were held on 11 and 12 January 2024, and the ICJ delivered its Order on Provisional Measures on 26 January 2024.

## Legal Questions Before the Court

1. **Jurisdiction and Admissibility:** Does the ICJ have the power to hear this case about genocide under the relevant treaty, and is South Africa allowed to bring this case to the ICJ?

2. **Plausibility of Genocidal Conduct:** Are Israel’s acts in Gaza reasonable attributable to genocidal intent under Article II of the Genocide Convention?

3. **Urgency and Risk of Irreparable Harm:** Do the facts justify the indication of provisional measures to prevent the escalation of the genocide?

4. **Standard of Proof at the Provisional Stage:** What threshold must be met to impose _interim obligations_ (agreements that cover a temporary period) on a state accused of genocide?

## Arguments

### South Africa’s Arguments:

South Africa alleged that Israel’s conduct, including the indiscriminate airstrikes, destruction of homes, schools, hospitals, and vital infrastructure, as well as the deliberate obstruction of humanitarian aid, amount to acts of genocide under Article II (a)-(c) of the Genocide Convention.

Evidence collected by South Africa included statements made by senior Israeli officials, such as references of “erasing” Gaza and dehumanizing language used by political and military officials  (Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in the Gaza Strip, Verbatim Record, 2024).

South Africa underlined in the hearings that, even in the absence of final proof of genocide, provisional measures were justified by the urgency and risk of irreparable harm to the Palestinians.

### Israel’s Counterarguments:

Israel denied committing or intending genocide, asserting that its operations were legitimate acts of self-defense against Hamas, a Palestinian political-religious extremist organization recognized by Israel as a terrorist group responsible for the October 7 attacks.

Israel highlighted efforts to mitigate civilian harm, (e.g. evacuation warnings), and accused Hamas of using human shields.

Israel argued that South Africa had misrepresented facts, and its case distorted international law by treating Israel’s war conduct as genocidal without sufficient legal basis  (Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in the Gaza Strip, Verbatim Record, 2024).

### The Court’s Considerations

The Court found that it had _prima facie_ jurisdiction under Article IX of the Genocide Convention, because both states are parties to the Convention and, according to Article IX, disputes over the interpretation of the Convention must be submitted to the ICJ. The Court reaffirmed the standard established in _The_ Gambia v. Myanmar, holding that provisional measures may be indicated if the rights in question are plausible, and there is a risk of irreparable prejudice and urgency  (The Gambia v. Myanmar, 2020).

On this basis, the Court concluded that at least some of the rights South Africa sought to protect were plausible, particularly the right of Palestinians in Gaza to be protected from acts of genocide, and that there was a risk of irreparable harm  (Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in the Gaza Strip (South Africa v. Israel) Provisional Measures, 2024). However, the Court declined to express any definitive opinion at this stage regarding whether genocide had occurred.

## Genocidal Intent

The Court did not confirm (nor deny) whether there was actual intent to commit genocide, but it found that some of the actions in question could potentially qualify as genocide under international law. Due to this possibility, the Court agreed that South Africa’s case had enough merit to move forward.  It examined the statements made by Israeli officials and the scale and impact of Israeli military operations in Gaza (number of deaths, destruction of infrastructure). As a result, the Court viewed that there was a real and imminent risk of irreparable prejudice to the rights protected by the Convention, sufficient to justify provisional measures.

## Interpretation of the Genocide Convention

The Court emphasized that the Convention confers standing on all States parties (_erga omnes_). Obligations under the Convention include prevention and punishment, even if genocide has not yet been proven. The rights and obligations invoked are plausible under the Convention.

## Provisional Measures, Urgency vs. Merit

The ICJ pointed out that it does not need to rule on the merits (whether genocide actually took place) at that stage. Instead, it focused on whether it appears to have jurisdiction, whether the rights claimed are plausible, and whether there’s an urgent risk of serious harm. The humanitarian situation in Gaza has been described as catastrophic, with a risk of further deterioration. While it acknowledged some steps taken by Israel to improve the situation, these were not considered enough to remove the danger. As a result, the conditions for granting provisional measures are satisfied.

## Distinguishing Genocide from Other Crimes

By not deciding whether a genocide has taken place in Gaza and by highlighting the fact that not all international crimes, such as war crimes and crimes against humanity, qualify as genocide, the Court subtly distinguished between the two. However, it stated that some allegations plausibly fall within the scope of the Genocide Convention. Because of that, it was necessary to take temporary protective measures while the case continues.

## Relevant Case Law & Precedent

The ICJ has clarified key legal principles through past cases, such as Bosnia v. Serbia (2007) where it confirmed that genocide, while Croatia v. Serbia (2015) allowed this intent to be inferred from conduct. In the Gambia v. Myanmar (2020), the Court held that a possible right and risk of irreparable harm are enough to justify provisional measures.

## Provisional Measures Issued (26 January 2024)

On 26 January 2024, the ICJ ordered Israel to adopt all necessary measures to prevent acts of genocide, ensure humanitarian aid, and report on compliance, though it did not order a ceasefire.

Provisional measures are urgent and temporary orders issued by the ICJ to protect the rights of parties in a dispute and prevent irreparable harm while the case is ongoing. They are binding and must be complied with, even though they do not determine the outcome of the case.  To issue provisional measures, the ICJ assesses: (1) _prima facie_ jurisdiction, that is, does the court have the official power to make judgements and decisions, (2) plausibility of the rights claimed, (3) the link between the measures (e.g. a ceasefire) and the rights that are being claimed, are they clearly connected? And (4) urgency and risk of serious harm.

## Legal Principles Clarified

●  At the provisional stage, the Court did not need to determine the occurrence of genocide. It suffices that the right to be protected from genocide is _plausibly at risk._

●  The duty to prevent genocide extends beyond the borders of a state. It includes the conduct of its agents abroad.

● Provisional measures under Article 41 of the ICJ Statute, _even though they are interim in nature_, are _binding_.

_The legal consequence of non-compliance is not yet issued._

## Limitations of the ICJ

Although the ICJ has been important in shaping international legal standards for how genocide is addressed, and the provisional measures under Article 41 of the ICJ Statute discourage harmful actions (genocide) by influencing the behavior of states, the ICJ has no direct power to enforce its ruling. Even though it can issue decisions and provisional measures, it relies on states to comply voluntarily.  While the ICJ can issue important rulings, its world impact is limited because it cannot force countries to obey, especially without broader UN support.  

## Political Impact

This case reflects the evolving interpretation of genocide and the ICJ’s growing role in international accountability. Its proceedings highlight the global significance of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, in both political and legal realms. Raising questions regarding international responsibility when dealing with war crimes.
