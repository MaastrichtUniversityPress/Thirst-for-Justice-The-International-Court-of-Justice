# 6. Application of the Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment

(Canada and the Netherlands v. Syrian Arab Republic)

## Historical Context

In 2000, Bashar al-Assad assumed the presidency of Syria, succeeding his father, Hafez al-Assad who modernized the country but at the cost of a brutal repression. When coming to power, Bashar promised the Syrian population that his leadership would differentiate from that of his father. However, this did not last (Bayley, 2015). His regime quickly turned into a dictatorship governing Syria as a totalitarian police state with systematic human rights abuses. The large-scale violations of international law started 11 years later with the beginning of the civil war which was triggered by the “Arab Spring” protests, a wave of pro-democracy protests that swept across Arab countries in late 2010 and throughout 2011 (Arab Spring, 2025).

Numerous rebel groups emerged during this conflict, which only had in common their objective of overthrowing the Assad regime. Many of these groups were known for abuses, including the killing and displacement of civilians. As a result, the President labeled this opposition as “terrorist”. A war followed between the rebel groups and the government, claiming the lives of thousands of Syrians.

After 24 years in power, the current regime finally collapsed in early December 2024 following a military campaign by the group Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (hereinafter, HTS) which seized control of the governmental institutions, and now claims governmental authority as its leader, Ahmed al-Sharaa became officially interim president in late January 2025.

However, HTS is designated as a terrorist group by both the United Nations and the European Union (Lennon, 2024). This sparked debate about governmental authority under international law. Despite the terrorist designation of this group and the unconstitutional takeover, many states initiated engagement with the new government due to their effective control of the Syrian territory and its institutions. This abrupt change of government has raised numerous questions about the recognition of this new government, its obligation and legitimacy and most notably about the case brought by Canada and the Netherlands against Syria for the violations of human rights under Assad's regime.

For the time being it remains unclear who will be representing Syria in front of the ICJ and whether this new regime will accept or challenge the responsibility for crimes committed by its predecessor.

## Facts

Since 2011, Syria has repeatedly committed violations of international law. In February of the same year, “Arab Springs” protests broke out in the country about economic, social and other human rights issues, with demands from the citizens for governmental reforms as well as calls for the release of political dissidents and activists who have been arbitrarily imprisoned (para. 26). The Assad regime responded with extreme violence to these protests. Facing these difficulties, the President introduced some reforms such as lifting the state of emergency which had been in place since 1963 and promising new laws on political parties and elections, however these never occurred.

In March 2011, a new wave of protest advocating for peace in the whole country started after the Syrian security forces tortured a group of Syrian children accused of defacing public buildings with anti-government graffiti in the city of Dar’a (“Syria: Crimes Against Humanity in Daraa,” 2020; para. 26). Once again, the authorities responded to this protest with arrests and attacks on demonstrators and activists.

The regime continued its violations with the use of chemical weapons to intimidate and punish the population, which resulted in numerous deaths, injuries, severe physical and mental suffering (para. 2).

In February 2020, a ceasefire was called in Syria, and the government regained significant territory (para. 6). However, even after this shift of circumstances, Syria failed to take measures to remedy its violations, which continue to this day.

## Applicants’ Claims

Due to the massive violations of international law and the impunity with which the authors carried on with continuity of these violations, The Netherlands and Canada decided to resort to the dispute settlement as contained in the Article 30(1) of the Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment.

In engaging in such proceedings, the Applicants hope for some reliefs where the court would adjudge and declare that Syria has indeed breached (and still breaches) its international obligations under the Convention against Torture in particular the Articles 2, 7, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, and 19; that Syria must accepts its state responsibility flowing from these internationally wrongful acts; that it must comply with those obligations and cease such violations; that it must provide measures guaranteeing a non-repetition of these violations; that it must punish those responsible of acts of torture and finally that it must provide all victims with full reparation i.e compensation and rehabilitation resulting in these violations (para 60).

Moreover, as a result of such breaches of international obligations, the court must determine the legal consequences that Syria would undergo (para. 61).

## Defendant’s Claim and Counterclaim of the Applicants

In response to these accusations, Syria has systematically denied wrongdoing despite the evidence. Moreover, it has defended its actions on the fact that they were necessary to combat terrorism (para 5).

Canada and the Netherlands have argued in return that these claims could not justify Syria’s actions, as states must still comply with international human rights law when combating terrorism.

## Admissibility of the Applicants’ Claim

For the ICJ to be able to judge this case, it must have jurisdiction under article 36(1) of the ICJ Statute, binding on the three states as UN members. Moreover, they are members of the Convention against Torture (CAT) and are therefore bound by Article 30(1), which lays down the jurisdictional requirements. These include a dispute, failed negotiations and no agreement on arbitration within 6 months. Syria has indeed failed to meet its obligations under the Convention and has refused to negotiate despite two years of attempts by the applicants.  Therefore, the ICJ should be able to exercise jurisdiction, as all the conditions set out are fulfilled (paras 8-24).

## Evidence of the Violations

The United Nations has established an Independent International Commission of Inquiry on the Syrian Arab Republic in order to investigate these accusations of violations of international human right law in Syria since 2011.

After carrying out its research, the Commission of Inquiry concluded that the Syrian government was indeed guilty of violations of international law, including arbitrary detention, torture and ill-treatment, and enforced disappearance to intimidate and punish political dissenters and their families (para 4).
