# 3. Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project

(Hungary/Slovakia) ICJ Judgment of 25th September 1997; Slovakia’s Request for additional judgment until 2017

## Facts

On 16th September 1977 in Budapest, Hungary and Czechoslovakia entered into a treaty to construct a series of dams and locks on the Danube River, known as the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project. The project's objectives included energy production, improved navigation, and flood control. However, differences arose between the Czech and Slovak Federal Republic and the Republic of Hungary regarding the implementation and the termination of the Treaty on the Construction and Operation of the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Barrage System.

In 1989 Hungary suspended and later abandoned its portion of the project, citing environmental concerns including adverse effects on water quality, wildlife habitats, and regional ecosystems. Conversely, Czechoslovakia emphasized the project's economic benefits, such as hydroelectric power generation and enhanced navigation. Later on in 1991, Czechoslovakia implemented a "provisional solution" (Variant C), diverting the Danube's waters to continue the project unilaterally. This solution was specifically the damming up of the Danube at river kilometre 1851.7 on Czechoslovak territory.

Hungary then formally terminated the Treaty in 1992. The States were unable to resolve the issues arising through negotiation and thus the delegations of the States expressed their commitments to submit the differences connected to the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros project to the ICJ for binding international arbitration in 1993. The case centered on the interpretation and application of the 1977 Treaty and related international environmental and treaty laws. It was found that the Slovak Republic was the sole successor State of rights of the former Czech and Slovak Federal Republic with regard to the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project.

In 1998 and facing Hungary’s unwillingness to comply with the judgment, Slovakia filled in the Registry of the Court a request for an additional judgment to enable the compliance with the judgment. In 2017 this situation was still pending, when on the 30 of June, Slovakia requested the discontinuance of the proceedings, to which Hungary agreed.

## Key Issues

The Court was requested to decide its judgement based on the Treaty and rules and principles of general international law, as well as any other treaties it may have found applicable. The key legal issues the court was asked to adjudicate on are as follows:

1. Was Hungary entitled to suspend and subsequently abandon its obligations under the 1977 Treaty due to environmental concerns?

2. Was Czechoslovakia (and later Slovakia) entitled to implement the "provisional solution" (Variant C) unilaterally?

3. What were the legal effects of Hungary's termination of the 1977 Treaty?

The Court was also required to determine the legal consequences, including the rights and obligations for each Party, arising from its judgement on the legal issues raised above. The Parties agreed that, upon the Court issuing a final judgement on the matter, they would establish and implement a temporary water management regime for the Danube. They further accepted the judgement of the Court as final and binding upon them.

## Applicant’s Claim

Hungary requested the Court to declare that the Republic of Hungary was entitled to suspend and subsequently abandon the works on the Nagymaros Project and on behalf of the Gabčíkovo Project for which the Treaty attributed responsibility to the Republic of Hungary. They also asked that the Court declare that the Czech and Slovak Federal republic was not entitled to proceed to the ‘provisional solution’ in the Treaty. Finally, they requested that the Court declare that by Hungary’s Declaration on 19th May 1992, it validly terminated the Treaty on the Construction and Operation of the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Barrage System of 16th September 1977 (Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), 1997, para. 13).

Hungary further argued that the Treaty of 16th September has never been in force between itself and the Slovak Republic (Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), 1997, para. 13(1)). It argued additionally that the Slovak republic was internationally responsible for the consequences of damage and loss for Hungarian nationals and the Republic caused by implementation of the provisional solution and thus under an obligation to make reparations (Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), 1997, para. 13(3)).

Hungary ask the Court to further declare that the Slovak Republic was under the following three obligations: first to return the waters of the Danube to their course along the international frontier between the Republic of Hungary and the Slovak Republic; second, to restore the Danube to the situation it was in prior to the putting into effect of the provisional solution; and third, to provide appropriate guarantees against the repetition of the damage and loss suffered by the Republic of Hungary and its nationals (Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), 1997, para. 13(5)). Hungary additionally argued that unforeseen environmental risks constituted a state of necessity and rendered the project's completion impossible, justifying suspension and termination of the treaty obligations.

## Defendant’s Claim

Slovakia requested that the Court declare that the Treaty between Czechoslovakia and Hungary of 16th September 1977 concerning the construction and operation of the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (to which the Slovak Republic is the acknowledged successor) is a treaty in force and has been from the date of its conclusion. They argued therefore that the termination by the Republic of Hungary on 19th May 1992 was without legal effect.

The Republic of Slovakia also requested that the Court declare that the Republic of Hungary was not entitled to suspend and subsequently abandon the works on the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project. That the act of proceeding with and putting into operation Variant C, the provisional solution, was lawful.

They further requested that the Court declare that the Republic of Hungary must therefore cease all conduct which impedes the full implementation of the 1977 Treaty and that it must take all necessary steps to fulfil its own obligations under the Treaty. Finally, it asked the Court to declare that in consequence of its breaches of the 1977 Treaty, Hungary is liable to pay, and the Slovak Republic is entitled to receive, full compensation for the loss and damage caused to the Slovak republic by those breaches (Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), 1997, para. 13).

## Court’s Considerations, Precedent, & Broader Implications

**Hungary's Suspension and Abandonment**

The ICJ examined whether Hungary's environmental concerns justified invoking a state of necessity or impossibility of performance to suspend and abandon its treaty obligations. Additionally, Hungary argued that the environmental risks that emerged from the project permitted suspension under Article 61 or Article 62 of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties. However, the Court rejected these claims, considering that:

a) The ecological concerns did not fulfil the criteria for invoking a state of necessity under customary international law, particularly as Hungary had contributed to the circumstances it claimed constituted necessity.

b) Supervening impossibility of performance was not established because Hungary could have fulfilled its obligations using alternative means.

c) A fundamental change of circumstances did not apply since the essential basis of the treaty had not disappeared.

**Slovakia's Implementation of Variant C**

The Court assessed the legality of Slovakia's unilateral actions in diverting the Danube River and proceeding with the project without Hungary's consent. Slovakia (then Czechoslovakia) implemented Variant C, a modified version of the original project, arguing necessity and Hungary's prior breach of treaty obligations. The Court held that although Slovakia had the right to implement a provisional solution due to Hungary's suspension, it could not unilaterally put it into operation without prior notification and agreement with Hungary. Additionally, the diversion of the Danube constituted a breach of international law, specifically violating the principle of equitable and reasonable utilisation of shared water resources.

**Termination of the Treaty**

The ICJ evaluated the legal validity of Hungary's 1992 termination of the 1977 Treaty. Hungary's notification of termination had no legal effect, as none of the conditions under Article 60 (material breach) of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, Article 61 (impossibility of performance), or Article 62 (fundamental change of circumstances) were met. Thus, the treaty remained in force.

## Judgment

**Hungary's Actions:** The ICJ concluded that Hungary was not entitled to suspend and subsequently abandon its obligations under the 1977 Treaty based on environmental concerns, as the conditions for invoking a state of necessity were not met.

**Slovakia's Actions:** The Court determined that while Slovakia was entitled to implement a provisional solution, it was not entitled to put it into operation unilaterally without prior notification to and agreement with Hungary.

**Treaty Termination:** The ICJ held that Hungary's notification of termination of the 1977 Treaty did not have the legal effect of terminating the treaty, which remained in force.

## Legal Principles Established

**Environmental Concerns vs. Treaty Obligations:** The case underscored the complexity of balancing environmental protection with adherence to treaty obligations, reinforcing the principle that environmental concerns alone do not justify non-compliance.

**Unilateral Actions:** The judgment highlighted limitations on unilateral actions by states in implementing international projects, emphasizing the importance of cooperation and negotiation.

**State Responsibility:** The decision reinforced principles of state responsibility and conditions under which states can invoke necessity to justify non-compliance. Necessity must meet stringent requirements and that countermeasures must be proportionate and in accordance with international law.

**Historical Parallels:** The case is cited alongside other ICJ cases involving environmental disputes and treaty interpretations, such as _Pulp Mills on the River Uruguay (Argentina v. Uruguay)_, which addressed transboundary harm and sustainable development. Ultimately, the _Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project_ influenced later rulings on water law and environmental impact assessments.

**Political and Legal Significance:** The case highlighted challenges in resolving disputes from conflicting environmental and developmental interests, influencing future international negotiations and treaty formulations.
