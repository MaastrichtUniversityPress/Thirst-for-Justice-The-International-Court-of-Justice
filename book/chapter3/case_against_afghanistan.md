# 8. Potential Case Against Afghanistan for Violations of Human Rights

## Introduction

Upon the Taliban’s return to power in August 2021, their dismantling of two decades of progress in women’s rights sparked worldwide backlash. In response, Australia, Canada, the Kingdom of the Netherlands, and Germany invoked Article 29 of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) against the Afghanistan’s de facto authorities (Huckerby, 2025). These countries have formally called upon Afghanistan to cease its violations against women’s human rights. If Afghanistan does not react, this initiative may have the result that Australia and the other countries seize the ICJ against Afghanistan, doing so in solidarity with Afghan women.

Afghanistan’s becoming a party to the CEDAW was historically progressive. As Farhoumand-Sims (2009) stated, “Afghanistan made history by becoming the first Muslim state to ratify CEDAW without ratifications” in 2003 (p.136). This ratification represented a binding pledge by the Afghan state to promote gender equality, regardless of the change in governmental authority.

But after they took power, the Taliban have systematically prevented the female population from exercising their fundamental rights. They have consistently restricted freedom of movement, education, speech, and employment. These escalating violations of the CEDAW have been issued over a series of decrees, going as far as prohibiting “women’s voices being heard in public” (European Parliamentary Research Service, 2024). These enshrined policies have been condemned globally, described as gender apartheid by various human rights groups and UN experts. Gender apartheid involves deliberate and structural oppression due to gender. To fight this injustice, 22 additional countries now support the legal proceeding that might be initiated against Afghanistan (Reid, 2024). This reinforces the importance of utilising international legal mechanisms to hold state authorities accountable and the international community’s commitment to doing so.

## Legal Framework and Procedure

Article 29 of CEDAW outlines the procedural framework by which state parties may bring disputes before the International Court of Justice. If no resolution regarding the interpretation and application of the Convention is reached using negotiations, a state may submit it for arbitration, which is the first step. In this case, the Afghan government has been formally notified of the claims, and 6 months have been given to the complainant to either respond or implement the necessary changes (Reid, 2024). Thus far, the Taliban have not demonstrated genuine attempts at implementing the necessary changes; the 4 states that have initiated the invocation may proceed to bring the case before the ICJ.

This case would aim to hold Afghanistan’s de facto government accountable for violating key provisions in the CEDAW:

● **Article 1** defines discrimination against women; the Taliban’s systematic exclusion of women from their fundamental rights constitutes a clear violation.

● **Article 2** outlines legislative and regulatory approaches to eliminating gender inequality, yet the oppressive policies in Afghanistan are enshrined in the Taliban’s decrees.

● **Articles 7, 10, and 11** entail the right to be educated, be employed, and have a voice, the Taliban have excluded these rights of women under their rule.

Critics argue that this case inadvertently would confirm the recognition of the Taliban as the legitimate representatives of Afghanistan (Wilmer, 2025). However, the CEDAW grants the ICJ jurisdiction over interstate disputes, and Afghanistan is still a member of the UN. Moreover, scholars have noted that the important issue is the safety of the people. Thus Professor Sivakumaran explains that the test to recognise certain acts of unlawful or unrecognised entities should be ‘whether failure to so recognize would be “to the detriment of the inhabitants of the [t]erritory”’ (Sivakumaran, 2009, 511). Legal proceedings do not result in the conferral of legitimacy to any involved unrecognised governments; rather, it would hold the Afghan state accountable for its treaty obligations.

## Timeline of Taliban Restrictions on Women

The European Parliamentary Research Service (2024) documents a visible escalation of restrictions decreed by the Taliban since their return:

● **August 2021:** Women ordered to stay home during 2021 takeover because “soldiers are not trained to respect women.”

● **September 2021:** Creation of the Ministry for the Promotion of Virtue and Prevention of Vice; banning girls from secondary education and employment. The Ministry of Women’s Affairs closed.

● **December 2021:** Women are prohibited from traveling unaccompanied by a male for more than 72 km.

● **May 2022:** Decree ordering women to cover their faces in public

● **November 2022:** Women are banned from public spaces, parks, gyms, and bathhouses

● **December 2022:** Women banned from university education; women prohibited from working in many NGO sectors

● **April 2023:** Afghan women banned from working with UN entities

● **May 2023:** Ban on female doctors registering for medical specialisation

● **August 2024:** “morality legislation” is published, codifying the restrictions decreed as well as introducing new ones, such as the prohibition of female voices in public

The order of decrees leading to the “morality legislation” demonstrates the systematic nature of the Taliban’s discriminatory oppression against women, providing clear evidence of gender apartheid.

Upon their return to government in Afghanistan, the Taliban are already facing international sanctions, such as asset freezes and travel bans (United Nations Security Council, 2021). The Afghan state will be subject to serious consequences depending on an ICJ ruling. Furthermore, Article 94 of the UN Charter states that ICJ rulings are binding on member states; failure to cooperate may trigger UN Security Council involvement, likely resulting in additional enforcement measures and putting more economic and political pressure on the de facto authorities to address their discriminatory actions.

A significant case such as this could also influence gender apartheid to be recognised as a legal concept under international law, supporting women in Afghanistan and the whole world. Treaty obligations like the CEDAW continue, despite changes in governmental authority, a principle that is important for human rights protection in politically unstable nations. This would introduce new and improved methods for addressing systematic gender discrimination across the globe. For the women of Afghanistan, domestic remedies are unavailable. Therefore this potential case gives hope for accountability and global recognition of their struggle.
