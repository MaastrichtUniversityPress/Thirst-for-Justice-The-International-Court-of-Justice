## 5. Application of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination

## Background of Conflict

The primary conflict of this case revolves around the Nagorno Karabakh region which is located in the Southeastern stretch of the Lesser Caucasus mountain range. The latter region has been subject to strong tensions and ongoing warfare between Armenia and Azerbaijan. Nagorno Karabakh has been internationally recognised as part of Azerbaijan, however historically it was populated by ethnic Armenians. In 1991, following the collapse of the Soviet Union, ethnic Armenians living in Nagorno Karabakh region declared and submitted independence from Azerbaijan. This formed a self-proclaimed and further unrecognized state that was called the “Republic of Artsakh”. The Republic of Artsakh was endorsed politically by Armenia, however it lacked recognition from any member state that was part of the United Nations (Ourfalian, 2020). This led to a war between Armenia and Azerbaijan (1988-1994). Russian intervention led to a ceasefire in the region in 1994 ending the war resulting in Armenian forces gaining control over the Nagorno Karabakh region. A border crisis was triggered on 12 May 2021, that occurred as a result of Azerbaijani soldiers crossing several kilometers into Armenian territory and consequently occupying between 50 and 215 square kilometers. Despite foreign intervention by the European parliament, the United States and France, the republic of Azerbaijan did not withdraw. The Largest attack occurred in September 2022, when Azerbaijan initiated its largest military offensive against the Republic of Armenia. On 28 September 2023, the Republic of Artsakh agreed to dissolve itself by the 1st January 2024, this effectively ended its existence as a political entity that presided in the Nagorno Karabakh region (Ourfalian, 2020).

## Admissibility and Claims of Both Parties

Armenia's claims that were submitted to the ICJ rested on allegations of ethnic cleansing that took place against Armenians which inhabited the Nagorno Karabakh region. Armenia's argument asserted that Azerbaijan actions led to the displacement and persecution of ethnic Armenians which would be classified under international law as ethnic cleansing (Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v. Azerbaijan),  2024, para. 25). Their second claim, issued by Armenia on the 16 of September 2021, alleged that the Republic of Azerbaijan violated the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (CERD), which was adopted on the 21 of December 1965. Armenia claimed that Azerbaijan breached and neglected the principal provisions that aimed at preventing and denouncing racial discrimination in all forms, including propaganda, access to justice and education and most importantly state responsibility (Articles 2,3,4,5,6, and 7 CERD). Armenia further asserted that Azerbaijan had violated its obligations under Paragraph 98 of the ICJ order of 7 December 2021 by:

(a) Failing to protect persons captured and detained in relation to the 2020 Conflict and hostilities that constitute a renewed flare-up of the 2020 Conflict,

(b)  Failing to ensure their security and equality before the law, particularly those who still remain in detention (_Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v. Azerbaijan),_ 2024, para. 25 ).

The third claim regarded humanitarian violations that were allegedly committed by Azerbaijan, which were carried out by blocking humanitarian aid (_Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v. Azerbaijan),_ 2024, para. 26). Lastly, Armenia claims that the Nagorno Karabakh ethnic Armenians should possess the right to exercise self determination and  thereby determine their own political status independently. Armenia argues that the right to self-determination remains a peremptory norm of international law, and must be followed and implemented.

On the other hand, Azerbaijan has persistently denied these allegations. It claims that its operations that are being carried out in the Nagorno Karabakh region are legitimate examples of sovereignty being exercised over its own internationally recognised territory. Azerbaijan further asserts that its actions are carried out with a goal to restore constitutional order and ensure equality and equal rights for all ethnic groups in the region. Azerbaijan lodged its own set of complaints to the ICJ against Armenia. The first being that Armenian forces have allegedly engaged in extensive landmine deployment that are found within Azerbaijani territories. Azerbaijan concludes that these actions highly endanger their own.

## Ruling of the ICJ on the Preliminary Objections in the case of Armenia v. Azerbaijan

On 21 April 2023, Azerbaijan filed two preliminary objections regarding the case initiated by Armenia at the International Court of Justice (ICJ) under the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (CERD)(Khachatryan, 2024). It contested the Court‘s jurisdiction on two grounds: firstly, claiming that Armenia had failed to meet the condition of negotiations set out in Article 22 of CERD and secondly stating that Armenia‘s claims were beyond the material scope (ratione materiae) of the Convention. The ICJ delivered its ruling on 12 November 2024, dismissing both preliminary objections (Nakajima, 2025).

## First Preliminary Objection: Failure to Meet the Requirement of Negotiations**

The Court began by mentioning its earlier ruling in _Georgia v. Russian Federation_, where it explained that “negotiation“ pursuant to Article 22 entails sincere discussions that concerns the dispute‘s subject matter, which must either collapse, become futile, or reach a deadlock before legal proceedings can begin (Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v. Azerbaijan), 2024, para. 52). In this instance, the Court determined that the discussions between Armenia and Azerbaijan genuinely addressed matters included in CERD and represented a sincere effort to negotiate (Khachatryan, 2024). It emphasized that Armenia did not have an obligation to reach an agreement with Azerbaijan but only had to show in good faith its intent to negotiate. The Court therefore rejected Azerbaijan‘s first preliminary objection and found the precondition of negotiations under Article 22 satisfied (Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v. Azerbaijan), 2024, para. 59).

## Second Preliminary Objection: Lack of Jurisdiction _Ratione Materiae_

With respect to the second objection, the ICJ stressed that it has to evaluate whether alleged acts can constitute violations of CERD, regardless of whether they might also come within the parameters of international humanitarian law (IHL).The Court affirmed that CERD remains applicable during armed conflict and can complement IHL. It concluded that acts such as murder, torture, and inhumane treatment targeting individuals on the basis of their Armenian ethnicity may amount to racial discrimination under CERD (Khachatryan, 2024).  Accordingly, the Court rejected Azerbaijan‘s second objection.

The case now proceeds to the merits phase, during which the ICJ will determine whether Azerbaijan has violated CERD (Application of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (Armenia v Azerbaijan), 2024, para. 68).

## Ruling of the ICJ on the Preliminary Objections in the case of Azerbaijan v Armenia

On 12 November 2024, the ICJ handed down its decision on three preliminary objections raised by Azerbaijan in response to the proceedings brought by Armenia under CERD.

Azerbaijan had accused Armenia of violating CERD, prompting Armenia to challenge the Court‘s jurisdiction on several grounds.

## First Preliminary Objection: Jurisdiction Ratione Temporis

Armenia‘s first objection concerned the temporal jurisdiction of the Court. The ICJ upheld its objection, affirming the principle of the non-retroactivity of treaties, a cornerstone of international law. The Court found that CERD only became binding on both Armenia and Azerbaijan from 15 September 1996 onward (Nakajima, 2025). Therefore, the ICJ ruled that it lacked jurisdiction over any alleged violations occurring prior to that date.

All of Azerbaijan‘s claims based on acts that took place before 15 September 1996 were dismissed. However, claims involving continuing acts that extended beyond this date remain within the Court‘s jurisdiction.

## Second Preliminary Objection: Jurisdiction Ratione Materiae Concerning Landmines

Armenia further contested the ICJ‘s authority regarding accusations about the deployment of landmines and booby traps. The Court, however, dismissed this objection as meaningless by stating that Azerbaijan was not presenting these actions as independent CERD violations. (Lando, n.d.). Instead, the allegations served as contextual evidence to support broader ethnic cleansing claims. The ICJ‘s jurisdiction to address landmine issues thus exists only within the context of racial discrimination claims and not as independent violations.

## Third Preliminary Objection: Jurisdiction Ratione Materiae Concerning Environmental Harm**

Finally, the ICJ upheld Armenia‘s third objection by determining that Azerbaijan‘s claims of environmental harm were outside the scope of CERD. The Court emphasized that CERD claims require racial discrimination as their foundational element. Azerbaijan, however, failed to demonstrate that the environmental damage was purposefully targeted at individuals on the basis of their Azerbaijani ethnicity (Nakajima, 2025).

Thus, Azerbaijan‘s claims on environmental damage were excluded from further proceedings under CERD.
