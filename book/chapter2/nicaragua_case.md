# 6. Nicaragua Case

Nicaragua v United States of America, Case concerning the Military and Paramilitary Activities in and Against Nicaragua [, Merits, Judgement, I.C.J Reports 1986, p. 14]

## Historical background

Following a period of US intervention in Nicaragua, the Head of the Nicaraguan National Guard assumed presidency in 1937, establishing a family dictatorship. The dictatorship began to fragment in 1972 due to an earthquake and mismanagement of foreign financial aid, creating revolutionary sentiments in the population. In 1979, the Sandinistas overthrew the President and a new government was installed, mainly composed of Sandinistas’ members (para. 18). In response, opponents of the new government formed the _Contras_, irregular military forces which received support by the US (para. 20). At the same time, Nicaragua provided arms to insurgent groups in El Salvador, with further evidence of Nicaraguan transborder incursions into the territories of Costa Rica and Honduras, contributing to regional instability and serving as justification for US intervention (Crawford, 2019, 2).

## Facts

In 1984, Nicaragua filed a complaint against the United States to the ICJ for violations of international obligations. The 1986 judgement established that a US government agency laid mines in Nicaraguan ports and waters (para. 80). US agents took part in operations against Nicaraguan oil installations and a naval base, and a US military aircraft infringed Nicaraguan air space (paras. 81, 86, 91). It was proven that the US did not create the contras, but largely financed, trained, armed and organised a subgroup. However, they were not under effective control by the US (para. 115).

The Court also confirmed evidence of Nicaraguan support for opposition groups in El Salvador and incursions into Costa Rica and Honduras (paras. 160, 164). El Salvador filed an application to intervene under Article 63 of the ICJ Statute, but the Court rejected the request, stating that El Salvador lacked direct legal interest (Crawford, 2019, 2).

## Key issues and position of the parties

The primary issue centred around US’ alleged violations of customary international law by using force against Nicaragua and infringing its territorial sovereignty. Nicaragua argued that the US violated its treaty obligations under Article 2(4) of the UN Charter by supporting the contras in recruiting, arming and financing their actions in and against Nicaragua. They further argued that the US had breached customary law by violating territorial sovereignty, using force against Nicaragua, intervening in their affairs and killing and kidnapping Nicaraguan citizens (para. 15).

The US claimed that the ICJ lacked jurisdiction and justified its actions under the principle of collective self-defence, referencing El Salvador, Costa Rica and Honduras (Crawford, 2019, 1).

## Judgement

In 1986, the ICJ ruled that the US had violated its obligations under international law, ordering cessation of unlawful actions and reparations to Nicaragua (para. 292). The Court found that their actions could not be justified under principles of collective self-defence, as no formal requests from other states were presented (Highet, 1987, 41). Additionally, Nicaraguan actions did not constitute an armed attack (Leigh, 1987, 206).

Several important legal principles were reaffirmed in the judgement, such as that the laying of mines and attacks on the infrastructure of a state constitute infringements of the prohibition of use of force (para. 227); arming and training military and paramilitary groups violates the principle of non-intervention (para. 292 (3)); the right of self-defence can only be invoked in response to an armed attack and must meet the requirements of proportionality and necessity; collective self-defence is permissible only at request of the affected state (paras. 194 - 199). The case also clarified that international obligations can be binding under both treaty law and customary international law (para. 179).

## Historiography

In its 1984 judgement, the ICJ rejected the US objections and declared the case admissible. The US did not take part in the proceedings on the merits. Article 53 of the Statute of the ICJ allowed the Court to continue with the case, even in the absence of the US, but set the obligation to maintain fairness and verify the validity of the claims presented. In the end, the US did not comply with its obligation of cessation of and reparation for their military and paramilitary activities in Nicaragua. By a court order in 1991, the proceedings for determining the amount of compensation to be paid by the US were suspended (Crawford, 2019, 2).

The Court’s decisions were largely supported by a vote of 12-3, with extensive dissenting opinions compiled on more than 500 pages (Leigh, 1987, 207). The case remains a landmark in international law, illustrating the ICJ’s authority and the limitations in enforcing judgments when powerful states refuse to comply.
