# 3. Corfu Channel Case

United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland v. Albania, “Corfu Channel case, Judgement of December 15th, 1949: I.C.J. Reports 1949, p. 244.”

## Historical Background

The facts of the case occurred at a very delicate time in history, during Cold War tensions, while the whole world was experiencing shifting power dynamics, especially in strategic regions such as the Corfu Channel, an important waterway. The incident that led to the judicial development of this case, reflected broader geopolitical concerns and suspicions among nations.

## Facts

On October 22nd 1946, two British destroyers (‘Saumarez’ and ‘Volage’) struck mines in the north part of the Corfu Strait, the Channel dividing Greece and Albania. The impact, occurred in Albanian waters, resulted in detrimental damage to the ships and to the on-board personnel, as forty-five British officers and sailors lost their lives, while forty-two were seriously injured. The UK alleged that Albania was responsible for the mines, either by planting them or permitting their placement by a third party, and therefore informed Tirana of its intention to sweep the Channel. Although Albania did not give consent to the operation, the sweep was still performed by the British Navy, who found and subsequently cut twenty-two mines

## Key issues and position of the parties

The main issue that needed to be addressed in this case was whether Albania placed the mines in the Strait or had knowledge of their existence. The second issue addressed was whether the UK violated Albania's sovereignty by entering the Channel on November 12th/13th and conducting the sweep without authorization. The United Kingdom argued that Albania was responsible for the mines, either through direct action or negligence, and sought compensation for the damages encountered. In turn, Albania denied responsibility and counter-claimed that the UK's mine-sweeping operation violated State sovereignty.

## Judgement

Three ICJ judgements were necessary, in order to reach a decision regarding the amount of compensation due to the United Kingdom from Albania, following the 1946 Corfu Channel incident.  

In the _First Judgement_ (ICJ Reports, 1948, p.15)_,_ of 25 March 1948, the Court established Albania's responsibility for the placement of the mines. Subsequently, the State challenged the Court's jurisdiction, which in the first judgement rejected the former’s arguments, asserting that Albania's non-participation did not prevent a decision (art. 53 ICJ Statute).  

In the _Second Judgement_ (ICJ Reports, 1949, p.4) of 9 April 1949, the Court addressed the merits of the case and found Albania responsible for the explosions, not directly for laying the mines but for having knowledge of them. The court also ruled that the UK's mine-sweeping operation was a violation of Albanian sovereignty.  

In the _Third Judgement_ (ICJ Reports, 1949, p. 244), 15 December 1949, the ICJ awarded £843,947 in compensation to the United Kingdom. The figure comprehended the replacement cost of the Saumarez (the more damaged ship), repairs to the Volage, and compensation for loss of life and injuries among naval personnel.

## Historiography

This case was essential for developing international law principles as it laid the first details for State Responsibility. It established that States are responsible for events within its territory and for having knowledge about a dangerous situation and failing to act in that regard, even without direct proof. Moreover, the case affirmed the right of innocent passage through international straits and upheld the principle of State sovereignty. However, debates on the case persist. Professor James Crawford, former ICJ judge, argued that the principle was meant to be applied only to international straits and believed the case has been overextended beyond its original scope (Crawford, 2013, pp. 70-86).
