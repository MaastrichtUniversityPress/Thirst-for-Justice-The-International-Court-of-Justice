# Conclusions of Chapter 2

This chapter has provided an overview of the most important historical cases of the ICJ, following the development of the Court’s jurisprudence and the evolution of customary international law. Without these cases, contemporary international law and politics would not have the answers to crucial questions, such as state liability, accountability, and jurisdiction.

However, as illustrated by cases such as _Nicaragua_ and _Serbia v Bosnia_, the reader should keep in mind that the Court’s decisions still give rise to great academic debate, raising the following question: Does the ICJ administer real justice, or is its role to display symbolic legal power?

As long as states with strong political influence are able to fail to respect the Court’s decisions - such as the US in _Nicaragua_ - without experiencing real consequences, there remain doubts about the actual capacity of the ICJ for doing justice in concrete situations. That said, earlier cases like _Lotus_ demonstrate that symbolic authority can at times result in concrete outcomes and real justice.

Although the non-compliance of some states attracts much attention, it must be kept in mind that the great majority of the ICJ rulings is respected and enforced by states. Generally, the enforcement and functioning of international law depends on the compliance of all participants in the international community.

We hope that the reader has gained a deeper understanding of the Court’s seminal cases during its 20th century history, and of how the development of crucial international legal principles took place. Looking ahead to the next chapter, it contains contemporary and pending cases before the Court. With the help of this chapter 2, the reader will be better equipped to reflect upon and discuss international justice in the world today.
