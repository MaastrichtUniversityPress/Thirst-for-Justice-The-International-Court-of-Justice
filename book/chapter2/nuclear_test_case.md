# 4. Nuclear Tests Case

Australia v. France, Nuclear Tests Case Judgment, ICJ Reports 1974, p. 253.

## Historical Background

This case was brought before the ICJ in the 1970s, that is during the Cold War Period, a period in history when global nuclear tensions were heightened (Thompson, 2011). Since 1966, France had been conducting atmospheric nuclear testing in the South Pacific, specifically at Mururoa Atoll, a small island close to Australia. The Pacific nations, Australia and New Zealand, voiced international opposition to French nuclear testing since they were concerned about radioactive fallout and environmental degradation due to the nuclear testing. They were also concerned about the overall health of people residing closeby in the Pacific. Before the case was brought to the Court, both Australia and New Zealand made numerous diplomatic attempts to halt French tests, these attempts even included protests in the United Nations (Salmond, 1974). Already by the beginning of the 1970s, international opposition to atmospheric nuclear testing was increasing. In the 1960s the Partial Test Ban Treaty of 1963, that prohibited atmospheric testing, was signed by multiple nations (United Nations Treaty Collection, 1963). However, France was not one of the signatories to the treaty, so it continued its testing (U.S. Department of State, n.d.).

## Facts

Australia decided to go to the ICJ and seek a ruling in which the Court would declare the French nuclear testing illegal under international law. France refused to participate in the proceedings, but made some public statements instead in which the nation committed to end atmospheric testing after 1974. The Court was tasked with deciding whether France’s testing was in fact illegal under international law and ultimately whether the unilateral commitment to cease testing would render the case moot.

## Key issues and position of the parties

The key issue that the Court had to decide was whether France’s atmospheric nuclear tests in the South Pacific violated international law, particularly relating to environmental and transboundary pollution. Further, the court needed to decide France’s public unilateral commitments to cease atmospheric nuclear testing were legally binding under international law and would render the case moot (International Court of Justice, 1974, paras. 43-44).

Australia argued that the French tests violated principles of customary international law, which includes the prohibition of environmental harm. They claimed that the nuclear tests led to radioactive contamination which affected Australian territories. They also claimed that the testing breached the no-harm principle that prevents States from using their territories in ways which harm other States and that the ICJ would have jurisdiction based on France’s acceptance of the Court’s authority in previous cases (paras 11-13).

France refused to participate in the case and did not recognise the jurisdiction of the ICJ. Among other things claiming that the Court lacked jurisdiction, France also argued that nuclear testing was a vital element of national defense, and that the testing was conducted in such a manner that minimised environmental harm and radioactive fallout (para 18). France also unilaterally announced that it would end atmospheric nuclear testing in 1975 and switch to underground testing instead.

## Judgement

The Court ruled that the public statements made by France to cease testing created a legally binding obligation under international law. A State’s official public commitments can have legal force, even without a treaty (paras 55-56). The Court held that since France had already announced it would end testing publicly there would no longer be a legal dispute to solve. The ICJ reaffirmed that it only has jurisdiction when an active dispute exists and since France voluntarily committed to end testing, the dispute was no longer ongoing. Australia’s claim was dismissed as moot by the Court to these rulings. The unilateral declarations of France were held to be legally binding and no judgement was made on the legality of nuclear tests themselves.

## Historiography

This case established an important precedent in international law regarding unilateral declarations (Rubin, 1977). The ruling of this case has influenced later environmental law cases and discussions on State responsibilities for transboundary harm (ICJ, 1996, para. 29). Even though the case strengthened the role of unilateral declarations and them creating legal obligations under international law, it also showed how the Court is limited in addressing disputes when the respondent State refuses to participate (para 15). This case also remains important in discussions about the legality of nuclear testing, even though no judgement of such was made, and also on the rights of States to protect their territories from transboundary harm (Bodansky, 2010).
