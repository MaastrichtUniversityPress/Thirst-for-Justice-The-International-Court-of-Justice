# 5. Tehran Hostages Case

United States v. Iran, United States Diplomatic and Consular Staff in Tehran, Judgment, I.C.J. Reports 1980, p. 3.

## Historical Background

In the late 1970s, the Iranian opposition, led by Ayatollah Khomeini with the aim to end the Shah’s regime ruling as an authoritarian monarchy, gained momentum. In January 1979, massive protests across Iran led to the overthrow of the Shah’s government and his escape into exile (Buchan, 2013).

## Facts

In October 1979, the US government informed Iran that they had granted the Shah permission to enter the US from his exile in Mexico for medical treatment. Because of a previous attack on the US Embassy in Tehran in February 1979, which was stopped by the Iranian Revolutionary Guard, they requested Iran to provide increased protection for the Embassy, which Iran agreed to provide (para. 14-15). Nevertheless, the US Embassy was overrun, and all staff members were detained and kidnapped by an armed group during a demonstration on November 4th, 1979, with no Iranian security personnel present at the Embassy (para. 17). Days on into the attack, repeated calls for help were made from the Embassy to the Iranian Foreign Ministry, but no attempt was made by Iran to intervene or terminate the situation (para. 18). In response, the US filed an application before the ICJ on November 29th, 1979 (para. 1).

In December, the UN Security Council adopted a resolution calling on Iran to release all hostages and to peacefully resolve the remaining issues between them and the US which showed no effect (para. 28). On January 15th, 1980, the US filed its Memorial before the Court (para. 4) and broke off diplomatic relations with Iran in April (para. 31). Iran never filed a Counter-Memorial to the US’s claims (para. 4).

## Key issues and position of the parties

The issue before the ICJ was whether Iran had violated international law by failing to protect the US Embassy in Tehran and its diplomatic and consular staff during the armed attack.

The US declared Iran to be in violation of the 1961 Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (VCDR) and the 1963 Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (VCCR), which contain the immunities of diplomats and consular officials and the duty of a state to protect the officials and property of another state within its territory. According to the US, Iran was obliged to cease these violations and pay reparations (para. 8).

Iran did not formally take part in the proceedings but argued in a letter from December 9th, 1979, that the ICJ could not make a judgement on the attack alone because it represented only a secondary aspect of an “overall problem” involving more than 25 years of continual interference by the US in Iranian internal affairs (para. 10).

## Judgement

The ICJ established the admissibility of the judgement, not considering the attack on the US Embassy as something “secondary” due to the importance of the legal principles involved (para. 36), diplomatic immunity being fundamental for the integrity of the international legal order (Munson, 1981, p. 544). Since violations of the VCDR and VCCR were in question, which both allow a state party to invoke the respective treaty before the ICJ if other methods of diplomatic or legal resolution fail, the Court decided that it had jurisdiction over the case (para. 46-49).

The ICJ separated the events into the armed attack on the US Embassy of November 4th, 1979, and the series of events following the attack (para. 56). For the attack, the ICJ could establish no evidence that the militants acted on behalf of Iran, concluding that they acted in their private capacity (para. 58). Although a country is generally only responsible for the actions of its official authorities, declarations of state officials showed support for the militant’s conduct, and Khomeini announced the official government support on November 17th, 1979 (para. 73). Due to these declarations, Iran was responsible under Art. 11 of the draft Articles on Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts, which reflect customary law. Although the militants did not act on behalf of Iran, a sufficient link between the state and their private acts could be established for the state to assume liability (Chirwa, 2004, pp. 8-9).

Apart from this, the VCDR and VCCR obliged Iran to take sufficient steps to protect foreign diplomatic missions (para. 61-62), which it failed to do despite the attack on the US Embassy in February proving that it had the means to (para. 63-64, 68). Consequently, the ICJ established that Iran was in continuing breach of the VCDR and VCCR, engaging its responsibility under international law. The Court determined that Iran was obliged to immediately terminate the situation, release the hostages, return embassy property and make reparations to the US (para. 95).

## Historiography

The Tehran Hostages Case set an important precedent on the principles of diplomatic immunity and state responsibility. The ICJ’s judgement reinforced that diplomatic missions must be protected, and that a state is responsible for stopping and preventing even acts that have been committed by non-state actors in cases such as Iran’s inaction and later endorsement. Moreover, the hostage crisis is a primary example that shows the tension between law and politics in international relations. Being one of the most highly politicised cases to ever been brought before the ICJ, it has raised profound questions on the Court’s authority and responsibility to exercise its jurisdiction in highly politicized contexts (Rafat, 1981, p. 429).
