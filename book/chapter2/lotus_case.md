

# 1. The Case of the S.S. Lotus 1927

France v. Turkey, Judgement of the PCIJ in the Lotus Case, 7 September 1927
Lotus Case (France v Turkey) [1927] PCIJ Rep Series A No 10

## Historical background

The Lotus case, ruled by the PCIJ, is historically significant due to its foundation in the principles of state sovereignty, equality and jurisdiction. These concepts date back to the Peace of Westphalia, which symbolized the emergence of the modern sovereign state system and established the idea that States are independent and equal in handling their own affairs. Emmerich de Vattel further reinforced this in his _The Law of Nations_, asserting that all States, regardless of power or size, are equal under international law (Vattel, 1758/2008). This historical context is key to understanding the Lotus case and its emphasis on these foundational doctrines (von Bogdandy & Rau, 2006).

## Facts

On the High Seas, a collision occurred between a French vessel (Lotus) and a Turkish vessel (Boz-Kourt), resulting in the sinking of the Turkish ship and the deaths of eight Turkish nationals. The Lotus transported the ten surviving passengers to Turkey, where the French officer in duty, Lieutenant Demons found himself not just a sailor, but a defendant charged with manslaughter, sentenced to 80 days behind bars with a fine (paras 10-11).

## Key issue and position of parties

The government of France raised objections, asserting that Lieutenant Demons, as a national of France, should be tried in the French judicial system (para 11). Turkey claimed jurisdiction, citing its interest due to the death of its nationals. The PCIJ heard the case to determine whether Turkey violated international law by exercising jurisdiction over a foreign national for an incident occurring outside its territory.

## Judgement

The PCIJ ruled that Turkey hadn’t breached international law by instituting criminal proceedings against Lieutenant Demons. The Court’s reasoning centers on three key findings:

Firstly, the collision occurred on the High Seas, where jurisdiction is typically limited without an international agreement. The Court held that in the absence of specific international rules, States could exercise jurisdiction unless expressly prohibited. This reflected what came to be known as the Lotus principle (paras 23-24).

Secondly, the Court reasoned that a State has the ability to exercise its jurisdiction within its own sovereign territory, even if there is no specific rule of international law permitting it. Therefore, as a sovereign entity and within its own territory, Turkey had jurisdiction over the incident, namely passive personality jurisdiction, as the victims were its nationals (para 24).

Thirdly, the Court recognized that ships are under the jurisdiction of the flag State. However, if the conduct of one ship affects another State, that State may also claim jurisdiction (paras 23-24).

To tie these key findings together, the Lotus principle established that States are free to act unless there’s a prohibition under international law (para 27). In other words, States have sovereignty over their own territory and additionally are able to exercise jurisdiction over matters occurring on the High Seas unless an international agreement restricts this power.

While the Lotus case didn’t necessarily create new customary law, it became a landmark interpretation of State freedom under existing customary norms, particularly influencing new treaty developments such as Article 11 of the _1958 Convention on the High Seas_, that provides for collisions on the High Seas (Convention on the High Seas, 1958).

## Historiography

The Lotus case is still important today, and over the years experts have debated its impact, often questioning its strong support for State freedom, especially as later laws placed more limits through treaties and international agreements (von Bogdandy & Rau, 2006). Scholars remain divided on the enduring value of the Lotus principle: some argue it grants excessive discretion to States and undermines cooperation, while others maintain that it upholds an essential safeguard of sovereignty (Hertogen, 2015).

In modern international law, the case continues to be relevant, particularly in discussions about legal responsibility in areas like cybercrime, international disputes and maritime law. As the world becomes more connected, many question whether the Lotus principle that allows States to act freely unless restricted, still makes sense (Hertogen, 2015). This case demonstrates the dynamic nature of international law, showing how legal decisions from the past continue to shape the way States interact and handle international issues today.
