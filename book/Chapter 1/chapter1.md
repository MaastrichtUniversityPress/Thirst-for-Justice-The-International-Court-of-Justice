## Introduction

The International Court of Justice Court (ICJ) "finds," "affirms," "is convinced,"…the headlines of well-established news outlets seemed to be permeated by the Court’s actions, whose restricted tone may at some points anger the readers, but be the source of rejoice for others.  What does seem to be obvious is that the existence of an authority seemingly “hovering” above states, at places reprimanding, sanctioning or in appearance punishing them, may appear out of line with the expectations of sovereignty many link to the legal international order and its enforcement. Though increasingly important with regards to recent developments on the world stage, it is still a great unknown for the larger public. This booklet addresses the reason for the existence of the ICJ, and what better way to do that than by starting to understand its purpose through the historical lenses of this chapter.

While gaining acute importance on the world stage in recent years, the existence of the Court had long been a mystery to some, and its impact unfelt by others. As the UN’s primary judicial institution, the ICJ had nevertheless an everlasting legacy and contributed more to the institutionalised pacific settlement of disputes than any other attempts in the past in global politics. The successor to the Permanent Court of International Justice (PCIJ), the ICJ offered the breakthrough to achieve equality between sovereign member States in litigations. This materialised through the possibility to settle international disputes in front of an impartial and competent court. Such an innovation in the international legal order finally provided less powerful States, or otherwise unheard States to challenge even the most powerful actors or the international community in front of a non-biased world court.

Though one may tie the increased role of the ICJ to a proportional increase in cases requiring its mediation, it is rather the increased complexity of the cases and the possibility to litigate which remains the driving force behind its overwhelming presence. Traditionally unable to seek mediation due to imbalance of power between them and the wrongdoers, smaller states welcomed the creation of such an institution, which remains one of the greatest, if not the most important step towards achieving international justice and impartial judicial litigation at an international scale.

In light of the current geopolitical turmoil and issues proper to the 21st century, it is undeniable that more-and-more countries will seek the services provided by the ICJ. A shifting world order from a singular to a bipolar one, then succeeded by a multipolar power balance has led to an increased role of the ICJ with the millennial breakthrough, suggesting an ever-increasing role of this institution in the future.

Some States see the existence of the ICJ as a threat to national sovereignty, while others view it as necessary compromise to ensure global stability. At any rate, it is undeniable that the ICJ has delivered, and more surely will deliver, pivotal advisory opinions and render cardinally important judgements in order to achieve global justice, and thus serve its purpose.

Given the challenges listed above, it is imperative that the general public and the readers become acquainted with this institution. Recently the media seems to be obssessed, notably, on the Advisory Opinion requested by the UN General Assembly on the _Legal Consequences arising from the Policies and Practices of Israel in the Occupied Palestinian Territory, including East Jerusalem_ (ICJ, 2023). Thus, many have been first acquainted with the Court in light of the widespread attention devoted to the conflict in Gaza. However, its procedural workings, possible outcomes and most importantly, its history and purpose on the international stage and impact on the implicated States, remain obscure. The goal of this chapter is to illuminate those fundamental aspects of the Court.

### The Permanent Court of Arbitration

Though this chapter primarily addresses the history and work of the ICJ, it is also necessary to address the existence of its neighbour, the Hague-based, Peace Palace-located Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA).

Established in 1899 during the First Hague Peace Conference, its existence precedes that of the ICJ (established in 1945 by the ICJ Statute) as well as that of its predecessors, the PCIJ, established as the judicial institution of the League of Nations, which was founded in 1920. The First Hague Peace Conference was called by Tsar Nicholas II, and notably culminated in the establishment of the PCA through the _Convention for the Pacific Settlement of International Disputes of 1899._

The concept of global justice solutions is centuries old. However, the PCA was the first materialisation of a concrete institution able to receive cases from a variety of applicants. States, but also international organizations and even private parties as well as mixed arbitrations are admitted to the Court. It is to be noted that the PCA’s jurisdiction is strongly dependent on the consent of the parties.

The PCA was founded in 1899 in order to provide an effective frame of arbitration for a multiplicity of actors. Not only did this spearhead the institutionalization of global and unbiased justice, it materialised the will for justice of the countries signatory to its accession treaty (PCA, 1907) and it remains open for accession for any State willing to become a contracting party to the Treaty.

Established as the first intergovernmental organisation providing arbitration services, the PCA is not a court in the traditional sense. It is often seized by a consensus of the parties concerned, wishing to settle disputes in a peaceful manner. In order to do so, the PCA refers to an array of tools, notably including conciliation, mediation, and fact-finding (PCA, n.d.-a).

The powers of the PCA were further expanded in 1907, due to its immense success as a pioneer in its field. Indeed, in the span of the first decade of its existence, it administered 15 inter-State disputes (Feng, 2024), and notably the 1908 Grisbådarna arbitration between Sweden and Norway on issues of maritime boundaries (PCA, n.d.-b).

Following the First World War, it experienced a significant recession in cases. The PCA is deemed to have stayed idle until the 1990s due to the unfavorable political and geopolitical climate. This led to Gilbert Guillaume, to call the Court a “Sleeping Beauty”. Most possibly he alluded to the utopian concept tied to its inception, but a relative recession of effective usefulness due to an environment largely out of its control (Guillaume, 2007).

The PCA has in the last decades experienced a substantial rebirth, with an effective increase in cases brought in front of it for administration and remains a central pillar in international dispute resolution service-providing. Its administrative framework has left a substantive legacy in the world of international adjudication.

  

## The Rise and Fall of the Permanent Court of International Justice

The dire state of global affairs after the Great War compelled lawmakers and world leaders of the time to seek a peaceful manner of interstate dispute resolution. In this context, the League of Nations emerged from the Paris Peace Conference negotiations in 1920 and, alongside it, the PCIJ. However, the idea of international conflict adjudication was not novel in the 1920s, as some efforts toward it had already been made during the Second Hague Conference in 1907 (Fachiri, 1925). These efforts ultimately ended in the development of international arbitration instead (Fachiri, 1925).

The PCIJ was provided for in Article 14 of the Covenant of The League of Nations. The Court held its first sitting on the 30th of January, 1922 (International Court of Justice, n.d.), two years after the Paris Peace Conference concluded the Great War and established the League of Nations. However, the journey required for the establishment of the framework of international adjudication was not a simple one, and required additional meetings of the Council of the League of Nations. Most notably, on February 13th 1920, in St. James’s Palace in London, the members of the newly-formed Council discussed the specificities of their obligations under Article 14 of the Covenant (Fachiri, 1925). The most pressing questions were also the most practical, such as the place of the official seat of the court, the scope of its jurisdiction, the procedure and the number and appointment of judges (Fachiri, 1925).

Ultimately, the structure of the court was provided for in Chapter I of the Statute of the Permanent Court of International Justice. The Court consisted of fifteen elected, independent judges of “high moral character”(Permanent Court of International Justice, 1920). The election procedure for the members of the Court was described in Articles 4-12 of the PCIJ’s Statute. In summary, the procedure required an absolute majority of votes of the Assembly and the Council of the League of Nations for each candidate nominated by national groups. Furthermore, Article 9 required that individual nominees be sufficiently qualified in the field of law, and that the Court, as a body, be representative of “all forms of civilisation” and of the variety of legal systems it would represent.

The drafters of the Statute of the Court settled the matter of jurisdiction by defining the subjects which could have the so-called “right of access” and could therefore appear before it in Article 34. This article defined the parties with the right of access as only States or Members of the League of Nations, strictly barring the participation of all legal and natural persons.

The Court functioned between the years 1922-1939, relatively undisturbed but facing multiple challenges. As the first international court with general jurisdiction, it dealt with 29 cases and delivered 27 advisory opinions (International Court of Justice, n.d.). The most notable judgments of the court were delivered in landmark cases such as _The SS Lotus case_ (dealing with the matter of jurisdiction), _The SS “Wimbledon” case_ (on the  matter of freedom of navigation)_, The Factory at Chorzów case_ (State responsibility and reparations), and many more.

As the first of its kind, the Court faced many problems, the most prominent of which were the scope of its jurisdiction, State participation, diplomatic and political pressures and problems regarding the enforcement of the PCIJ's rulings.

Ultimately, these challenges, combined with the League of Nations' inability to prevent World War II led to the Court's dissolution. Alongside the League, the PCIJ was dismissed as well. Instead, there emerged a new and polished version - the International Court of Justice _(International Court of Justice, n.d.)_. But before moving to the ICJ let’s have a closer look to the PCIJ.

### Why a World Court?

The emergence of the PCIJ took place within a specific historical context, driven by the ambition to draw up an international legal framework. Its development was gradual, closely tied to earlier ideas and principles about international adjudication, preceding the establishment of any formal judicial body. This evolution particularly reflects the influence of the North American approach to international relations. The North American standpoint believed that law, and more specifically, the rule of law, should have a positive impact on international affairs (Hernandez, 2014).

After the Great War, people all over the world had a strong desire for peace, which is indeed reflected in the name given to World War I, “The War to End All Wars”. Establishing the PCIJ provided the world with hope of a new and more efficient way of handling interstate disputes. Indeed, the PCIJ intended to provide a more neutral legal method. The method would focus on law and not power, in contrast with the previous method of resolution which consisted of diplomacy and armed conflict (Bournemouth University, 2014). Moreover, with the re-awakening of global trade and state cooperation, like with the international organisation of the League of Nations, there was an increasing need for an impartial institution to adjudicate potential conflict in the fairer way possible (James, 2023).

Since the 1899 and 1907 Hague Conventions did not quite succeed, countries were pushed to try even harder to create an international court. During the Paris Peace Conference, with strong support from the U.S., Britain proposed a draft plan to set up a Permanent International Court, which would be substantially different from the “mere” Institution of Arbitral Justice envisaged previously (Hernandez, 2014).

As the French statesman Léon Bourgeois (1851-1925) stated in 1920, there was room for an international tribunal for administering international law in parallel with the national courts, the laws of which are limited to their territory. (Hernandez, 2014). Léon Bourgeois wanted the League of Nations to represent a power that would be the guardian of a law that could resist aggression. After the worldwide violence of the previous years, he believed that it was time for the rule of law to be instigated in the international sphere (Niess & Altinok, 2021).

The Council of the League of Nations, represented by five permanent members (the British Empire, France, Italy and Japan) and four non-permanent members, took up the idea of establishing a Court for the Organisation. It was soon included in Article 14 of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles. The creation of the PCIJ was treated as an urgent matter. Quickly, plans for an international court of justice were presented to the Assembly, and a Committee of Jurists arrived to the Hague in June 1920. After one month, the Committee provided the Draft Statute of the Court (Lloyd, 1985). The Court was closely linked to the League by being its fourth principal organ and acted as its judicial branch. Later, Léon Bourgeois became the President of the Assembly of the League of Nations and maintained its will of a strong judicial organ, firmly believing that international justice was a cornerstone of lasting peace.

The designation of “Permanent” for the Court was meant to symbolise that the Court was a place where judges would always be ready to hear appeals from countries and complaints about violations of rights. The initial idea was to show that the PCIJ was not just for solving disputes, but was truly a Court of Justice.

## PCIJ during The Second World War

### Pre-War Activity and Sudden Suspension

The first permanent international court was a turning point in the development of international law (Gorman, n.d). The PCIJ continued to operate in its last years. The _Electricity Company of Sofia and Bulgaria (Belgium v. Bulgaria)_, its final case, concerned a contract dispute between a Belgian business and the Bulgarian government (Permanent Court of International Justice, 1939). Even though it was operating normally at the time, tensions on a global scale were quickly rising.

The Court’s operations were abruptly stopped in May 1940 when Germany invaded the Netherlands. The judges were unable to convene due to the occupation of The Hague, and unable to communicate with the League and other international actors (Sarooshi,1999). Although the Court could not function, it was not formally dissolved. Judges were either trapped by travel restrictions related to war, fled or returned home. Despite existing on paper, the PCIJ essentially stopped functioning in that moment (JusMundi, n.d).

### Wartime Inactivity and Legal Paralysis

Between 1940 to 1945, the PCIJ was completely dormant. German soldiers literally occupied the Peace Palace. No hearing was conducted, no opinion delivered and no administrative functions were performed. This effectively resulted in it being a court in name only. The machinery of the law was brought to a halt and proved that international law was not applied during the war (Refworld , n.d). During the war, the overwhelming majority of the League Member States were under occupation or at war.

No genuine attempt was made during the war to revive or maintain the Court. The judges were dispersed, the Court staff could not convene, and the Court did not enjoy significant political backing. The interwar period demonstrated the vulnerability of international judicial bodies to global crises.

### Dissolution and Successor’s Institution

The United Nations was established by the Allied Powers to replace the defunct League of Nations after WWII ended in 1945. The ICJ was created as the UN’s new principal judicial body in accordance with Article 92 of the UN Charter (United Nations, n.d).

The ICJ inherited the PCIJ’s procedures, location and archives and in certain cases, personnel (JusMundi, n.d), even though it was a new organisation in theory. The PCIJ was disbanded in April 1946. Since the ICJ started operating in the same Peace Palace, the transition went smoothly. However, the consequences of the five years of total inaction were notable: the PCIJ had not adjusted to the realities of war. It failed to endure the strains of international conflict in spite of its early successes.

### The ICJ from the End of the Cold War Until Now

The end of the Cold War allowed for international relations to resume to their normal functioning. This ease of tensions on the international stage is reflected in the workload of the ICJ. The Court issued twice as many judgements a year between 1990 and 2012: 2.72 judgements a year on average, as it did between 1946 and 1989: 1.18 judgements a year on average. (International Court of Justice, 2012). This increase in cases brought before the Court, led to challenges regarding the ICJ’s functioning that it still has to face nowadays. As the Court is acquiring a gradually more prominent role on the international stage, one may ponder on the extent to which its rulings impact international relations, as well as the ways its efficiency may be improved.

Some of the main challenges the ICJ has to face concern jurisdiction. States are sovereign entities on the international stage, and the Court only has jurisdiction when States have given it authority to issue rulings on specific subject matters. In a word, the Court can only adjudicate on a certain conflict when the States concerned allow it. This constitutes a limit to the efficiency of the Court and to its ability to have a say in international disputes. A State aware of its international responsibility for a breach of international law might decide not to give the Court jurisdiction. Not only will the State then not be held accountable, but the Court will further see its role diminished as international adjudicator. This first general issue related to jurisdiction is derived from the principle of sovereignty and can hardly be remedied.

A second jurisdictional issue is linked to the functioning of the Court and to the ways in which consent can be given. Indeed, consent to jurisdiction can happen in a number of ways.

Firstly, states can agree to submit a specific dispute to the jurisdiction of the Court through a compromise or special agreement (art. 36(1) ICJ statute).

Secondly, the parties can also rely on a compromissory clause, a treaty provision that confers on the Court the jurisdiction to solve disputes relating to their application.

Thirdly, the Court might have jurisdiction through the optional clause procedure, according to which states can file a declaration with the UN Secretary General that they recognise the Court’s jurisdiction as compulsory in relation to certain matters (art. 36(2) ICJ Statute) (International Court of Justice, n.d.).

When the statute of the PCIJ was drafted, it was expected that the optional clause procedure would have a central role in accepting the Court’s jurisdiction, yet, the use of Compromissory clauses became the favoured way of bringing disputes to the Court. Since its creation, 61 cases have been brought to the Court on the basis of compromissory clauses, whereas only 27 cases used the optional clause procedure. Nowadays, there appears to be a decrease in the inclusion of compromissory clauses in treaties. Multilateral treaties are becoming less common, and rarely include such clauses. This could threaten the efficiency of the Court as it would reduce the scope of its jurisdiction and lead to a reduction of the number of cases brought before it (International Court of Justice, 2021).

Moreover, the Court might be faced with the refusal of a State to comply or even recognise its ruling. This is yet another issue linked to state sovereignty and to the autonomy of States. While there exists mechanisms to push States to comply with rulings, these can fail (Schulte, 2004). It must however be noted that non-compliance is rare and doesn’t render a judgement useless: it may still be beneficial for one of the parties and have an impact on the international community (Schulte, 2004). An example of such an instance is the Nicaragua case, as detailed below in Chapter 2.

Another challenge faced by the Court lies in its dual-role of independent judicial body and of primary legal organ of the United Nations. The ICJ must strive to keep its autonomy while having many links with the United Nations. The Court is financially dependent on the UN: its expenses are part of the expense of the organisation and its budget is subject to the Security Council’s approval. Furthermore, its judges are elected by the General Assembly and the Security Council, which could present an obstacle to their independence. Under the wrong circumstances, these links could undermine the Court’s impartiality (Gowlland-Debbas, n.d.).

Lastly, some scholars are of the opinion that the Court still has the structure of an arbitral court, and should be modernised to adapt to today’s international environment. Today’s international stage is drastically different from that of 1945, when the ICJ was created, let alone from that of 1920, when the PCIJ was established. One important evolution is the emergence of a number of international organisations. Some argue that they should also benefit from the existence of an International Court of Justice. Therefore scholars have suggested changes to open up the Court’s contentious jurisdiction to non-state actors, such as international organisations or to open the Court’s advisory functions to more international organisations  (Cassese, 2012).

Another arbitral characteristic of the Court that some wish to strip, is the possibility for a state to appoint an ad hoc judge in a dispute where that state is not already represented in the conflict (art. 31 ICJ Statute). While this feature is meant to ensure fair representation of all parties in the rulings, it takes away from the Court’s impartial adjudicative role and gives it the conciliatory feature of a court of arbitration (Cassese, 2012). Other proposals for modernisation include the setting up of a fact-finding body or measures to streamline the internal functioning of the Court for greater efficiency (Cassese, 2012).

As society evolves, new international disputes and worldwide issues appear. The Court must therefore adapt to these and treat gradually more diverse cases. An example of this is the rather new issue of climate change litigation. The Court then plays a vital role in the legal recognition of the climate crisis and of responsible parties. Its judgements have already led to many legal advances. In its judgment in the case concerning _Certain Activities Carried Out by Nicaragua in the Border Area_, the Court affirmed that international law requires compensation for damage to the environment. In _Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons_, it noted that “the environment is not an abstraction but represents the living space, the quality of life and the very health of human beings, including generations unborn”. In the Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros Project, it acknowledged that respect for the environment concerns “the whole of mankind”; as mentioned above,  in the _Advisory Opinion on Obligations of States in Respect of Climate Change_ the Court has declared climate change ‘an existential problem of planetary proportions that imperils all forms of life and the very health of our planet.’ While progress remains to be made, this shows that the ICJ can occupy a key-role in the fight against climate change and in the issues derived from it (International Court of Justice, 2021). This will be further detailed in the analysis of the Advisory Opinion on climate change in Chapter 3.

### The Objectives of the Court and its Structure

The structure, functions, and objectives of the Court have evolved through a variety of historical events in order for the Court to take its current shape. In its final shape, the ICJ seats a total of 15 judges, who are elected regardless of their nationality (art. 2 ICJ Statute). No two current judges can hold the same nationality (art. 3 ICJ Statute).

There is also a long-standing convention that there should be judges of the nationality of all 5 Permanent Members of the Security Council (Hernández, 2022, p. 337). The judges are elected independently (art. 8 ICJ Statute) by the General Assembly and the Security Council, through the nomination of national groups. (art. 4 ICJ Statute)  The candidates receiving the absolute majority votes are elected as judges (art. 10 ICJ Statute).

The Court mainly serves two functions: contentious and advisory (Hernández, 2022, p. 339).

The contentious jurisdiction focuses on the disputes between states, submitted to the Court by states (art. 34 (1) ICJ Statute) that accept the jurisdiction and established rules of procedure of the Court (Hernández, 2022, p. 338).  States can provide their consent towards the jurisdiction of the Court in the four following situations according to the Article 36 of the ICJ Statute:

1) The case can be referred to the court by agreement of the parties, 
2) the matter might be specifically mentioned in the United Nations Charter, 3) the conflict might have arisen from a treaty that contains a clause that refers any conflict to the court, and 4) the states can directly consent to the jurisdiction of the court.

However, states may also challenge the Court’s jurisdiction through a procedure called the Preliminary Objection. During this procedure, states can base their objections on the competence of the Court, the time of which the dispute occurred, and the dispute not falling under the jurisdiction of the ICJ (Hernández, 2022, p. 344).  The decision of the Court, while it is exercising its contentious jurisdiction, is binding upon the state parties.

On the other hand, the Court’s advisory function focuses on answering the questions of certain institutions and bodies when such a question is directed towards the ICJ. However, the ability to direct a question to the Court is limited to specific international organizations. While the General Assembly and the Security Council are competent to refer any legal question to the Court, other UN organs may only request advisory opinions on issues within the scope of their purpose (art. 96 UN Charter). The two most significant differences between the two functions of the Court are that advisory opinions cannot be requested by states, and the decisions on the advisory opinions are non-binding.

Through this structure and functions, the Court works towards achieving some explicit and implicit objectives. Firstly, and most obviously, the Court is the primary judicial organ of the United Nations and therefore has the main objective of settling disputes and advising bodies within the working of the United Nations, according to international laws and principles (art. 92 UN Charter).

Throughout its existence, the Court aimed to promote the peaceful settlement of disputes between states and to make a future armed conflict between states less likely. The Court also has implicit objectives that are common for international courts in the generalized sense. These objectives are to promote compliance with the international norms, facilitate the operation of the related regimes, and confer legitimacy on the political system that established the Court, which is in this case the United Nations (Shany, 2012, p. 244-247). Lastly, by its position and authority in the international legal order, the Court takes on the objective to be the leading force for the development of newly emerging international rules and norms (Hernández, 2014).
