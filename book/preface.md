# Preface

This open textbook, on the International Court of Justice, adds to the abundant literature that testifies to the enduring scholarly fascination amongst international lawyers with the work of the principal judicial organ of the United Nations, the only permanent generalist court of the international legal order, and for perhaps too many, the great temple of international legal argument, whence we draw inspiration for the toolbox and vocabulary of what ‘counts’ as international law. I am honoured to write this brief Preface to introduce this Booklet, particularly because of its bottom-up approach and student contributions to engage with a wider public on the inner workings of the Court.

In her Introduction, Dr Mónica García-Salmones aptly captures the central paradox of the Court and its functioning: a cornerstone institution of the United Nations legal order, yet all too often perceived as remote from public view. The highly mediatised proceedings regarding the requests for provisional measures by South Africa against Israel, in _Application of the Genocide Convention to the Gaza Strip_, (January 2024) were perhaps a turning point in recent years. To this we might add a renewed wave of advisory requests from the General Assembly, ranging from State obligations to prevent climate change, to the functioning of ICAO and UNRWA, and also turning to the long-standing occupation of the Occupied Palestinian Territory.

These recent proceedings have generated ever more interest from the general public in the Court’s work and inner workings, and for a new generation encountering international law for the first time through the Court, has inspired them to produce this booklet. The chapters you now hold take up this question directly, and are the fruit of a commendable educational enterprise: the ICJ authors’ course at the University of Maastricht in Spring–Summer 2025. Twenty-five talented students, divided into four teams, took on the daunting task of engaging with the Court’s history, its landmark cases, and the emerging challenges of our time. This booklet makes accessible for a broader readership what is often reserved for international legal scholars and experts: the institution of the ICJ, its judicial function, its authority; and its limits.

Much of my own early and recent research agenda has been driven by an interest both in the the law-making, adjudicative and normative capacities of the ICJ. To do so is not merely a question of doctrinal or technical interest, nor simply of tracing Court’s ‘recent developments’ in action. These lines of inquiry have led me to a conviction: the ICJ remains vital, not because it is flawless, but because it operates at the intersection of law, politics and global order. As I once wrote about the Court, its case law is primarily a mirror reflecting the evolving architecture of public international law; but in recent years, I pose the question anew: is it emerging as its engine?

For me, the answer remains much the same: the Court matters precisely because it remains a forum in which law, consent, power and principle converge. It offers States a mechanism of adjudication and advice; it forms part of the architecture through which global law is forged; and it embodies, however imperfectly, an aspiration of collective legal governance beyond individual State sovereignty. International lawyers, for their part, are as a group transfixed by the Court: its judgments are too often seen as the final word on many an issue, and through more than a century of case law, the Court wields informal—and very real—authority within the international legal order.

To understand the Court in its context, and why it is reified by so many international lawyers whatever their methodological predilections, is to understand the history of, and current state of thinking in, the international legal discipline. Beyond our discipline, the real measure of the Court’s significance lies less in its pronouncements than in how States, institutions and civil society translate them into action.

The chapters ahead invite you to join in this engagement: to understand how the ICJ functions as an institution, to travel through its seminal jurisprudence, and to peer into its future-oriented potential. Whether you are a newcomer to the Court or have previously dipped into its decisions, I hope this booklet provides you with the tools and some insights to make an informed judgment about justice, peace and politics in an unstable, yet inevitably globalised world.

_Gleider Hernández1, Professor of Public International Law and Head of Department of International and European Law, Catholic University of Leuven (KU Leuven); immediate past President (2023-2025) of the European Society of International Law._
